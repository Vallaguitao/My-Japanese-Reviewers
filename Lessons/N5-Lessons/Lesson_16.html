Grammar 1a

V₁ て, [V₂ て,] V₃

Sequential Actions
📐 Structure
V₁ て-form, [V₂ て-form,] V₃ (final verb sets tense)

📖 How it works

When describing two or more actions that happen in sequence, list them in chronological order using the て-form. Only the last verb in the chain carries the tense (ます/ました). The て-form verbs before it are "neutral" — they just connect. This is like using "and" or "and then" in English to link a series of events.

V₁ て
→
V₂ て
→
V₃ ます/ました
📝 Example ① — Morning routine
朝あさ ジョギングを して、シャワーを 浴あびて、会社かいしゃへ 行いきます。
In the mornings, I go jogging, take a shower, and then go to work.
📝 Example ② — Past tense chain
神戸こうべへ 行いって、映画えいがを 見みて、お茶ちゃを 飲のみました。
I went to Kobe, saw a movie, and drank some tea.
💡 Only the last verb sets the tense! In example ②, 飲みました (past) tells us the entire chain happened in the past. The て-form verbs (行って, 見て) don't carry tense — they just link. If you change the last verb to 飲みます, all three actions shift to present/habitual.

Grammar 1b

Adjective て-forms

い-adj 〜くて / な-adj 〜で

📖 Linking Descriptions

Just as verbs use the て-form to chain, adjectives have their own て-form for linking multiple descriptions about the same subject. The rules are different for い-adjectives and な-adjectives, following the same pattern distinction that runs through all of Japanese grammar.

📝 Example ③ — い-adj linking
ミラーさんは 若わかくて、元気げんきです。
Mr. Miller is young and energetic.
📝 Example ④ — い-adj linking (past)
きのうは 天気てんきが よくて、暑あつかったです。
It was fine and hot yesterday.
📝 Example ⑤ — な-adj linking
ミラーさんは ハンサムで、親切しんせつです。
Mr. Miller is handsome and kind.
⚠️ Note: 〜て cannot link contrasting descriptions!

The adjective て-form is only for descriptions of the same subject that go together naturally. For contrasting descriptions (small BUT clean), you must use が (L8): × 部屋へやは 狭せまくて、きれいです。 → ○ 部屋へやは 狭せまいですが、きれいです。

Grammar 1c

N で — Linking Noun Sentences

N₁ で、N₂

📖 Nouns link the same way as な-adjectives

When connecting two noun sentences about the same subject, the first noun takes で — identical to the な-adjective linking pattern. This makes sense because nouns and な-adjectives share the same copula system (です/じゃありません/でした).

📝 Example ⑥ — City description
奈良ならは 静しずかで、きれいな 町まちです。
Nara is a quiet, beautiful city.
📝 Example ⑦ — Identity + status
カリナさんは インドネシア人じんで、富士大学ふじだいがくの 留学生りゅうがくせいです。
Karina is Indonesian; she's a student at Fuji University.
📝 Example ⑧ — Two people, two facts
カリナさんは 学生がくせいで、マリアさんは 主婦しゅふです。
Karina is a student, and Maria is a housewife.
💡 Linking summary — all three types: Verbs: V₁ て、V₂ (L14 て-form). い-adj: 〜い → 〜くて. な-adj / Nouns: 〜で. All three use a form of て/で to connect — this is the great unifying pattern of Japanese linking!

Grammar 2

V₁ てから、V₂

"After doing V₁, then V₂"
📐 Structure
V₁ て-form + から、V₂

📖 How it works

While V₁ て、V₂ simply lists actions in order, V₁ てから、V₂ explicitly marks V₁ as a precondition or preparatory step for V₂. Think of から as adding emphasis: "AFTER doing V₁, THEN V₂." V₁ is often something that must be completed before V₂ can happen. The tense of the whole sentence is determined by the final verb. And when a different subject does V₁, that subject is marked with が.

📝 Example ⑨ — Instruction sequence
お金かねを 入いれてから、ボタンを 押おして ください。
Put the money in, and then press the button.
💬 Example ⑩ — Lunch plan
A: もう 昼ひるごはんを 食たべましたか。
Have you already had your lunch?
B: この 仕事しごとが 終おわってから、食たべます。
I'm going to eat when I've finished this job.
🔗 V₁ て、V₂ — Simple sequence
食たべて、寝ねました。
I ate, then went to sleep.
Just "and then" — time order
⏩ V₁ てから、V₂ — Precondition
食たべてから、寝ねました。
After eating, I went to sleep.
"AFTER" — V₁ must finish first
💡 Subtle difference: V て、V = "I did X and Y." V てから、V = "AFTER doing X, I did Y." てから emphasizes completion of the first action as a precondition. It's like the difference between "I ate and slept" vs "After I ate, I slept."

Grammar 3

N₁ は N₂ が adj

Characteristics
📐 Structure
N₁(topic) は N₂(characteristic) が adj

📖 How it works

This pattern describes a characteristic or quality that the topic (N₁) possesses. N₁ is the overall topic (set by は), and N₂ is the specific aspect being described (marked by が). Think of it as zooming in: "As for [topic], [specific thing] is [adjective]." This creates sentences like "Osaka's food is delicious" or "Maria has long hair."

📝 Example ⑪ — Place characteristic
大阪おおさかは 食たべ物ものが おいしいです。
The food in Osaka is very good.
📝 Example ⑫ — Famous for
ドイツの フランケンは ワインが 有名ゆうめいです。
Franken in Germany is famous for its wine.
📝 Example ⑬ — Person's feature
マリアさんは 髪かみが 長ながいです。
Maria has long hair.
💡 は + が double-particle pattern: は sets the BIG topic ("Speaking of Osaka..."). が zooms into the SPECIFIC aspect ("...the food is..."). This is NOT the same が as L9's feeling-object が (好きが) — this が highlights which part of the topic is being described. You'll see this は...が pattern across many future structures.

L9 N₁ は N₂ が 好すき / 上手じょうず — Topic has feeling toward object

L13 N₁ は N₂ が 欲ほしい — Topic wants something

L16 N₁ は N₂ が adj — Topic has characteristic (NEW)

All share the same structure: は (big topic) + が (specific focus). The adjective/verb after が determines the meaning: feeling (L9), desire (L13), or characteristic (L16).

Grammar 4

N を V — Starting Point

出発点しゅっぱつてん
📐 Structure
N(starting point) を 出でます / 降おります

📖 How it works

Certain verbs like 出でます (leave/go out) and 降おります (get off) use the particle を to mark the starting point — the place you leave or exit from. This is a special use of を, different from its usual role as the object marker (L6). Think of it as "passing through" or "departing from" a location.

📝 Example ⑭ — Leaving home
7時じに うちを 出でます。
I leave home at seven.
📝 Example ⑮ — Getting off a train
梅田うめだで 電車でんしゃを 降おりました。
I got off the train at Umeda.
💡 を for movement through/out of a space! This を marks a place you DEPART from: うちを 出でる (leave home), 電車でんしゃを 降おりる (get off the train). Compare with に for ARRIVING: バスに 乗のる (board the bus, L15). を = exit point, に = entry point — they're complementary!

L6 N を V — Direct object ("eat a bento," "read a book")

L16 N を V — Starting/departure point ("leave home," "get off train")

L23 N を V — Route/path ("turn at the corner," "cross the bridge")

を expands from object (L6) → departure (L16) → route (L23). All share a thread: を marks what the action "passes through."

Grammar 5

どうやって

"How do you ~?"

📖 How it works

どうやって is used to ask about the way or method of doing something, especially how to get somewhere. It literally means "doing it how?" (どう = how, やって = doing). It's perfect for asking about transportation routes or methods for accomplishing tasks. The answer typically uses sequential て-form chains!

💬 Example ⑯ — Getting to the university
A: 大学だいがくまで どうやって 行いきますか。
How do you get to the University?
B: 京都駅きょうとえきから 16番ばんの バスに 乗のって、大学前だいがくまえで 降おります。
I take the Number 16 bus from Kyoto Station and get off at Daigakumae.
💡 Notice how everything connects! The answer to どうやって uses: バスに 乗のって (L15: board with に) → 大学前だいがくまえで 降おります (L16: get off). Plus て-form chaining (L16) to link the steps. This one question-answer pair uses grammar from L5, L14, L15, and L16 together!
Grammar 6

どれ / どの N

"Which?" (3 or more)

📖 How it works

どれ is used to ask "which one?" when choosing from three or more items. When modifying a noun directly, use どの + N instead. This connects to the こそあど system (L2/L3) and contrasts with どちら (L12), which is for choosing between exactly two items.

💬 Example ⑰ — Which umbrella?
A: ミラーさんの 傘かさは どれですか。
Which is Mr. Miller's umbrella?
B: あの 青あおい 傘かさです。
It's that blue one.
💬 Example ⑱ — Which person? (with description)
A: サントスさんは どの 人ひとですか。
Which is Mr. Santos?
B: あの 背せが 高たかくて、髪かみが 黒くろい 人ひとです。
That tall, black-haired man.
💡 どれ vs どの: どれ stands alone as a pronoun ("Which one?"). どの must be followed by a noun ("Which [noun]?"). どれ cannot modify a noun directly — just like これ (this one) vs この (this [noun]).
