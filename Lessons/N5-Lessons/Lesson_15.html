Grammar 1

V て も いいですか

"May I ~?" — Permission
📐 Structure
V て-form + も いいですか

📖 How it works

This is the standard way to ask for permission in Japanese. も means "even" and いいです means "it's fine," so the literal meaning is "Even if I [do this], is it fine?" It's polite enough for most everyday situations — classrooms, offices, restaurants, and more. The て-form from L14 connects directly into this pattern.

📝 Example ① — Taking a photo
写真しゃしんを 撮とっても いいですか。
May I take a photo?
💬 Example ② — Smoking (Permitted)
A: ここで たばこを 吸すっても いいですか。
May I smoke here?
B: ええ、[吸すっても] いいですよ。
Yes, you may [smoke].
💬 Example ③ — Smoking (NOT Permitted)
A: ここで たばこを 吸すっても いいですか。
Is it all right to smoke here?
B: いいえ、[吸すっては] いけません。禁煙きんえんですから。
No, you can't. It's a No Smoking area.
💡 Softening a refusal: When withholding permission, a direct いけません can feel harsh. A common softer response is: すみません、ちょっと…… (Sorry, it's a bit...) or giving the reason: のどが 痛いたいですから。 (I have a sore throat.) These are more natural in everyday conversation.
Grammar 2

V ては いけません

"You must not ~" — Prohibition
📐 Structure
V て-form + は いけません

📖 How it works

This pattern expresses prohibition — something that is not allowed. は marks the action as the topic, and いけません means "it won't do / it's no good." Think of it as "As for [doing this action], it won't do." It's used for rules, regulations, and instructions. Note that this expression carries authority — it's used when you have the position to prohibit someone (teacher → student, sign → public), not from someone of lower status to higher.

📝 Example ④ — No smoking
ここで たばこを 吸すっては いけません。禁煙きんえんですから。
You can't smoke here; it's a No Smoking area.
✅ Permission — ても いいですか

Grammar 3a

V て います — States

状態じょうたいをあらわす

📖 Expanding て います from L14

In L14, て います meant "is [currently] doing" (an action in progress). Now L15 reveals its second major meaning: describing a state that resulted from a past action. The action happened before and its result continues now. This is used mainly with verbs in the ～て います form where the focus is on the resulting condition, not the ongoing activity.

📝 Example ⑤ — Marriage state

📝 Example ⑧ — Residence
わたしは 大阪おおさかに 住すんで います。
I live in Osaka.
⚠️ Critical: Negative of 知しって います!

The negative is 知しりません — NOT × 知しって いません. This is because 知しる is about the moment of learning (an instantaneous action), while 知しって います describes the resulting state of knowing. Since you can't be "in the middle of not knowing," the negative returns to the simple ません form. This is the #1 most tested exception in JLPT N5!

💬 Example ⑨ — Do you know?
A: 市役所しやくしょの 電話番号でんわばんごうを 知しって いますか。
Do you know the City Hall phone number?
B: はい、知しって います。/ いいえ、知しりません。
Yes, I do. / No, I don't.

Grammar 3b

V て います — Habitual Actions

習慣しゅうかん・職業しょくぎょう

📖 Third meaning: Habits & Occupations

て います also describes habitual or repeated actions — things someone does regularly or professionally. When used with occupation-related verbs, it expresses what someone does for a living. When used with habitual actions, it describes what they do routinely over a long period. This is て います's third personality!

📝 Example ⑩ — Company activity
IMCは コンピューターソフトを 作つくって います。
IMC produces computer software.
📝 Example ⑪ — Store habit
スーパーで ナンプラーを 売うって います。
Supermarkets sell nam pla.
📝 Example ⑫ — Occupation
ミラーさんは IMCで 働はたらいて います。
Mr. Miller works at IMC.
📝 Example ⑬ — Ongoing study
妹いもうとは 大学だいがくで 勉強べんきょうして います。
My younger sister is studying at university.
💡 Three faces of て います by L15:
1️⃣ Action in progress (L14): 今いま 食たべて います = "is eating right now"
2️⃣ Resulting state (L15): 結婚けっこんして います = "is married"
3️⃣ Habitual action (L15): IMCで 働はたらいて います = "works at IMC"
Context determines which meaning! 今いま (now) → action. State verbs (know, live, marry) → state. Occupation/routine → habit.
⚠️ Notes

持もって います — Two Meanings

Note 2

📖 持もって います = "having" or "carrying"

The textbook's Note 2 highlights that 持もって います has two possible interpretations depending on context. It can mean you physically have something with you right now, or it can mean you own something (even if it's not with you at this moment).

📷 Ownership (State)
わたしは カメラを 持もって います。
I have a camera. (I own one.)
🎒 Physical Possession (Right now)
今いま カメラを 持もって います。
I have a camera on me. (Carrying it.)
💡 Japanese doesn't have a single "have" verb! Unlike English where "have" covers ownership, possession, and more, Japanese uses: あります (existence, L10) for things that exist somewhere, 持もって います (L15) for things you own or carry, and います (L10) for living things. The concept of "having" is split across different patterns depending on what exactly you mean.

Grammar 4

N に V — Location of Result

結果けっかの場所ばしょ
📐 Structure
Place に V

📖 How it works

The particle に is used with certain verbs to indicate the location where the subject ends up as a result of the action. These are typically verbs of movement that result in a position: entering, sitting, riding, boarding. Think of に as marking where you "land" or "settle." This is different from で (L6), which marks where you perform an action.

📝 Example ⑭ — Prohibition with に
ここに 入はいっては いけません。
You can't come in here.
📝 Example ⑮ — Permission with に
ここに 座すわっても いいですか。
May I sit here?
📝 Example ⑯ — Instruction with に
京都駅きょうとえきから 16番ばんの バスに 乗のって ください。
Take the Number 16 bus from Kyoto Station.
VerbMeaningWhy に?Future Lesson
入はいりますenterResult: you're in the placeL15
座すわりますsitResult: you're at the seatL15
乗のりますride / boardResult: you're on the vehicleL16

💡 に vs で — The critical distinction: に marks where you end up / exist as a result. で marks where you actively do something. バスに 乗のる = board a bus (result: you're ON the bus). バスで 行いく = go BY bus (means of transport). Same noun, different particles, different meanings!
Grammar 5

N₁ に N₂ を V

Place an Object at a Location
📐 Structure
N₁(place) に N₂(thing) を V

📖 How it works

This extends Grammar 4 by adding an object: you're placing or fixing something at/on/in a location. The に marks the location where the object ends up as a result of the action. This is the same "resulting location" に, now with a direct object.

📝 Example ⑰ — Stopping a car
ここに 車くるまを 止とめて ください。
Stop the car here, please.
📝 Example ⑱ — Writing an address
ここに 住所じゅうしょを 書かいて ください。
Write your address here, please.
💡 に = destination/target of placement. ここに 車くるまを 止とめる = stop the car [so it ends up] HERE. ここに 住所じゅうしょを 書かく = write the address [so it appears] HERE. The に points to where the result lands.

に vs で — The Key Distinction

最もっとも 大切たいせつな違ちがい
📍 に — Resulting Position

バスに 乗のる = board a bus (→ you're ON the bus)

いすに 座すわる = sit on a chair (→ you're IN the chair)

ここに 書かく = write here (→ writing APPEARS here)

Focus: where the subject/object ends up

🔧 で — Action Location

バスで 行いく = go BY bus (transport)

公園こうえんで 食たべる = eat IN the park (activity)

大学だいがくで 勉強べんきょうする = study AT university (activity)

Focus: where the action takes place / the means

⚠️ Most tested particle pair on JLPT! に vs で is the #1 particle confusion point. Remember: に = endpoint/target/result. で = action-zone/means. When in doubt, ask: "Am I describing where something ends up (に) or where I'm doing something (で)?"

知しる — The Trickiest Verb

N5 最重要さいじゅうようポイント
AffirmativeNegative
Question知しって いますか。 (Do you know?)
Yesはい、知しって います。—
No—いいえ、知しりません。
⚠️ NOT—× 知しって いません
💡 Why? 知しる is an instantaneous verb — it describes the moment of "coming to know." 知しって います captures the resulting state of having learned something ("I know"). Since you can't be "in the state of not having learned," the negative doesn't use て いません — it goes back to the simple 知しりません ("I don't know"). This is tested on EVERY JLPT N5 exam!
