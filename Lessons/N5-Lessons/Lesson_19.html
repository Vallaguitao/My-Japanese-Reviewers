Grammar 1

V た-form

Plain Past — The 4th Verb Form

📖 The Easiest Conjugation You'll Learn

The た-form is the plain past tense of verbs. Here's the great news: if you already know the て-form (L14), you already know the た-form! The rule is beautifully simple — just swap て→た and で→だ. That's it. No new rules to memorize, no new exceptions. Every verb follows this pattern perfectly.

💡 Master rule: て→た, で→だ. That's the entire conjugation! 買かって → 買かった. 読よんで → 読よんだ. 話はなして → 話はなした. If you drilled て-form in L14, た-form is free!

L14 て-form — Neutral connector (requests, states, linking)

L17 ない-form — Plain negative (obligations, prohibitions)

L18 Dictionary form — Plain affirmative (abilities, "before")

L19 た-form — Plain past (experience, representative actions)

With all four forms, you now have the complete toolkit for plain-form grammar. These power nearly every N5 and N4 pattern!

Grammar 2

V た ことが あります

"Have done ~ before"
📐 Structure
V た-form + ことが あります

📖 How it works

This pattern is used to talk about life experiences — whether or not you have ever done something at some point in your life. It does NOT specify when the experience happened. Think of it as "Have you ever ~?" in English. The verb always takes the た-form, and こと (like L18) turns the verb phrase into a noun meaning "the experience of doing ~."

📝 Example ① — Life experience
馬うまに 乗のった ことが あります。
I've ridden a horse (before).
⚠️ Experience (ことがあります) vs Specific Past (ました)!

た ことが あります = general experience, no time specified ("I've ridden a horse at some point in my life"). V ました = specific past event, often with time details. Compare example ① with example ②:

📝 Example ② — Specific past event
去年きょねん 北海道ほっかいどうで 馬うまに 乗のりました。
I rode a horse in Hokkaido last year.
💡 When to use which: "Have you ever eaten sushi?" → おすしを 食たべた ことが ありますか。"I ate sushi yesterday." → きのう おすしを 食たべました。The experience pattern asks about life, not a specific day!

Grammar 3

V₁ たり、V₂ たり します

"Do things like ~ and ~"
📐 Structure
V₁ た-form + り、V₂ た-form + り します

📖 How it works

This pattern lists representative actions from a larger set — "I do things like X and Y (among other things)." It's non-exhaustive, meaning there are other actions not mentioned. The particle や (L10) does the same thing for nouns; たり does it for verbs. The tense is determined by the final します/しました. Each verb takes its た-form + り.

📝 Example ③ — Habitual
日曜日にちようびは テニスを したり、映画えいがを 見みたり します。
On Sundays, I play tennis, watch a film, and so on.
📝 Example ④ — Past
日曜日にちようびは テニスを したり、映画えいがを 見みたり しました。
Last Sunday, I played tennis, watched a film, and did some other stuff.
🔗 V₁ て、V₂ て、V₃ (L16) — Complete sequence
日曜日にちようびは テニスを して、映画えいがを 見みました。
On Sunday, I played tennis and then watched a film.
Exhaustive list, time order, sequential
🎲 V₁ たり、V₂ たり します (L19) — Examples
日曜日にちようびは テニスを したり、映画えいがを 見みたり します。
On Sundays, I do things like playing tennis and watching films.
Non-exhaustive, no fixed order, representative
⚠️ Don't confuse て-form chaining with たり～たり!

て-form chain (L16) = listing ALL actions in order. たり (L19) = picking SOME actions as examples from a longer list, with no time relationship between them. You wouldn't use たり for routine actions everyone does (wake up, eat, sleep) — that's L16's territory.

Grammar 4

～く / ～に なります

"Become ~" — Change of State

📖 How it works

なります means "to become" and indicates a change of state. How you connect to なります depends on the word type: い-adjectives use くなります, な-adjectives and nouns use に なります. This pattern describes transitions — getting cold, getting better, turning 25, and so on.

い-adjective

〜い → 〜く なります
寒さむい → 寒さむく なります
Get cold

な-adjective

〜[な] → 〜に なります
元気げんき[な] → 元気げんきに なります
Get better

Noun

N → N に なります
25歳さい → 25歳さいに なります
Turn 25 (years old)
📝 Example ⑥ — い-adj change
寒さむい → 寒さむく なります。
It gets cold. (The weather becomes cold.)
📝 Example ⑦ — な-adj change
元気げんき[な] → 元気げんきに なります。
Get better. (Become healthy/energetic.)
📝 Example ⑧ — Noun change
25歳さいに なります。
Turn 25 years old.
💡 Same rule as adjective て-forms (L16)! い-adjectives always transform via the く pattern (〜くて, 〜くない, 〜くなる). な-adjectives and nouns always use the に/で pattern. This consistency runs through ALL of Japanese grammar — once you see the い→く vs な→に split, you can predict how any new pattern will work!

💡 こと turns any verb into a noun! Dictionary form + こと = "the act of ~" (abilities, hobbies). た-form + こと = "the experience of having ~ed" (experiences). This nominalizer is one of the most powerful tools in Japanese — it lets you treat entire actions as things you can talk about!
