Grammar 1a

Noun Modification — Foundations

名詞修飾めいししゅうしょく

📖 You already know how to modify nouns!

Since Lesson 2, you've been placing words before nouns to describe them. This is the fundamental word order of Japanese — the modifier always comes BEFORE the noun it describes. What's new in L22 is that you can now use entire clauses (with verbs, adjectives, full sentences) as modifiers. This is one of the most powerful grammar tools in all of Japanese.

What you already know
ミラーさんの 家うち Mr. Miller's house (L2 — noun の noun)
新あたらしい 家うち A new house (L8 — い-adj + noun)
きれいな 家うち A beautiful house (L8 — な-adj + noun)
✨ NEW in L22 — Full clauses as modifiers!
ミラーさんが 住すんで いた 家うち The house where Mr. Miller lived
💡 The golden rule: In Japanese, the modifier always goes BEFORE the noun. Whether it's a single word (新しい 家) or an entire clause (ミラーさんが 住んで いた 家), the structure is always: [modifier] + NOUN. This is the opposite of English, where relative clauses come AFTER the noun ("the house where he lived").
Grammar 1b

Verb Clauses Modify Nouns

動詞どうしの修飾しゅうしょく

📖 All four plain verb forms work

Verbs in a modifying clause must be in plain form (dictionary, ない, た, なかった). The tense and polarity of the modifying clause is independent of the main sentence. Here's how all four forms modify the noun 人ひと (person):

Example ① — 京都きょうとへ ～ 人ひと
京都きょうとへ 行いく 人ひと a person who goes to Kyoto
京都きょうとへ 行いかない 人ひと a person who doesn't go to Kyoto
京都きょうとへ 行いった 人ひと a person who went to Kyoto
京都きょうとへ 行いかなかった 人ひと a person who didn't go to Kyoto
💡 Plain form acts as an adjective! Just like 新あたらしい 家うち (new house), 京都きょうとへ 行いった 人ひと is "Kyoto-went person." The clause becomes a giant adjective. Every plain form (L17–19) and L20's なかった all plug in here!
Grammar 1c

Adjective & Noun Modifiers

形容詞けいようしと名詞めいし

📖 Adjectives and nouns in modifying clauses

い-adjectives in modifying clauses stay as-is (they're already plain form). な-adjectives use ~な, and nouns use ~の — exactly as in L2 and L8. No changes here! But now you can combine them into longer descriptions.

Adjective modifiers
背せが 高たかくて、髪かみが 黒くろい 人ひと a tall, black-haired person
親切しんせつで、きれいな 人ひと a beautiful, kind person
Noun modifiers
65歳さいの 人ひと a 65-year-old person
Modifier TypeConnectorExample
Verb (plain form)— (direct)行いった 人ひと
い-adjective— (direct)高たかい 人ひと
な-adjective～なきれいな 人ひと
Noun～の65歳さいの 人ひと

L2 N の N — Simple noun modifier (ミラーさんの うち)

L8 い-adj + N / な-adj な N — Adjective modifiers (新しい うち, きれいな うち)

L22 Clause + N — Full sentence modifiers (ミラーさんが 住んで いた うち)

The principle is always the same: modifier comes BEFORE the noun. L22 just makes the modifier bigger!

Grammar 2

Modified Nouns in Sentences

修飾しゅうしょくされた名詞めいしの使つかい方かた

📖 The modified noun works like any normal noun

Once you build a [clause] + NOUN phrase, that entire unit functions as a single noun. It can be the subject (は/が), object (を), location (に/で), or fit into any pattern you already know. The textbook demonstrates this beautifully with one modifying clause in six different sentence positions:

📝 Example ② — As topic (は … です)
これは ミラーさんが 住すんで いた 家うちです。
This is the house where Mr. Miller used to live.
📝 Example ③ — As subject (は … 古ふるいです)
ミラーさんが 住すんで いた 家うちは 古ふるいです。
The house where Mr. Miller used to live is old.
📝 Example ④ — As object (を 買かいました)
ミラーさんが 住すんで いた 家うちを 買かいました。
I bought the house where Mr. Miller used to live.
💡 The modified noun is still just a noun! Once built, [ミラーさんが 住んで いた] 家 = "the Miller-lived-in house." It acts exactly like the word 家 by itself — you can add は, を, が, に, or any particle after it!
Grammar 2

More Positions

続つづき — 例れい⑤⑥⑦
📝 Example ⑤ — With 好すきです (が)
わたしは ミラーさんが 住すんで いた 家うちが 好すきです。
I like the house where Mr. Miller used to live.
📝 Example ⑥ — As location (に)
ミラーさんが 住すんで いた 家うちに 猫ねこが いました。
There was a cat in the house where Mr. Miller used to live.
📝 Example ⑦ — With ことが あります (へ)
ミラーさんが 住すんで いた 家うちへ 行いった ことが あります。
I've been to the house where Mr. Miller used to live.
💡 Six positions, one modifying clause! Examples ②–⑦ all use the same clause (ミラーさんが 住んで いた) modifying the same noun (家). The ONLY thing that changes is what comes AFTER the noun — the particle and main predicate. This proves the modified noun is completely flexible — it goes anywhere a regular noun goes.

Grammar 3

が Marks Subject in Clauses

は → が

📖 Why が, not は?

Inside a noun-modifying clause, the subject is marked by が (not は). This is because は marks the topic of the WHOLE sentence, but inside a subordinate clause (a sentence within a sentence), the subject takes が. This is one of the most important は vs が distinctions in Japanese.

📝 Example ⑧
これは ミラーさんが 作つくった ケーキです。
This is the cake that Mr. Miller baked.
📝 Example ⑨
わたしは カリナさんが かいた 絵えが 好すきです。
I like the picture that Karina painted.
📝 Example ⑩
彼かれが 生うまれた 所ところを 知しって いますか。
Do you know the place where he was born?
⚠️ Inside a modifying clause: ALWAYS use が for the subject!

× ミラーさんは 作つくった ケーキ → ○ ミラーさんが 作つくった ケーキ
は is for the main sentence's topic. Inside clauses, subjects take が. This is a key grammar rule tested on JLPT!

Grammar 4

V dict. + 時間じかん/約束やくそく/用事ようじ

Time / Appointment / Errand
📐 Structure
V dictionary form + 時間じかん / 約束やくそく / 用事ようじ + が あります／ありません

📖 How it works

This is actually noun modification in action! The verb in dictionary form modifies 時間じかん (time), 約束やくそく (appointment/promise), or 用事ようじ (errand/business). The V-dictionary form describes WHAT the time/appointment/errand is for. This lets you express "I have time to ~," "I have an appointment to ~," or "I have an errand to ~."

📝 Example ⑪ — No time to ~
わたしは 朝あさごはんを 食たべる 時間じかんが ありません。
I don't have time to eat breakfast.
📝 Example ⑫ — Have an appointment to ~
わたしは 友達ともだちと 映画えいがを 見みる 約束やくそくが あります。
I've arranged (to see) a film with some friends.
📝 Example ⑬ — Have an errand
きょうは 市役所しやくしょへ 行いく 用事ようじが あります。
I have to do something at City Hall today.
💡 This IS noun modification! 食たべる 時間じかん = "eating time." 見みる 約束やくそく = "watching appointment." 行いく 用事ようじ = "going errand." The dictionary form acts as an adjective describing what kind of time/appointment/errand it is. L22's noun modification principle powers everything!

Grammar 5

V ます-form ましょうか

"Shall we ~?"

📖 A new use for an old pattern

You learned ましょうか in L14 as an offer to help ("Shall I ~?"). In L22's conversation, ましょうか is used to suggest doing something together ("Shall we ~?"). The form is the same — the meaning shifts based on context. When the speaker and listener will clearly do it together, ましょうか = "Shall we ~?"

🤝 L14 — Offering help
荷物にもつを 持もちましょうか。
Shall I carry your bags?
🏃 L22 — Suggesting together
今いまから 行いきましょうか。
Shall we go now?
📝 Example ⑭ — Apartment viewing
この 部屋へや、きょう 見みる ことが できますか。
Can I see this flat today?
……ええ。今いまから 行いきましょうか。
...Yes. Shall we go now?

L6 V ましょう — "Let's ~" (suggestion/invitation)

L14 V ましょうか — "Shall I ~?" (offering help)

L22 V ましょうか — "Shall we ~?" (suggesting doing together)

Same form, context determines meaning: offering to do FOR someone (L14) vs suggesting doing TOGETHER (L22).

Key Concept

English vs Japanese Order

語順ごじゅんの違ちがい
🇬🇧 English — Clause AFTER noun

the house where Mr. Miller lived

the person who went to Kyoto

the cake that he baked

🇯🇵 Japanese — Clause BEFORE noun

ミラーさんが 住すんで いた 家うち

京都きょうとへ 行いった 人ひと

彼かれが 作つくった ケーキ

💡 The key mental shift: In English, you say the noun first, then describe it ("the book that I read"). In Japanese, you describe first, then say the noun (読んだ 本). Japanese has NO relative pronouns (who, which, that, where) — the clause simply sits before the noun. This makes it cleaner once you get used to the order!

Caution

Common Mistakes

よくある間違まちがい
❌ Putting the clause AFTER the noun (English order)

× 人ひと 京都きょうとへ 行いった → ○ 京都きょうとへ 行いった 人ひと
Japanese modifiers always come BEFORE the noun. This is the #1 mistake English speakers make!

❌ Using は instead of が inside the modifying clause

× ミラーさんは 作つくった ケーキ → ○ ミラーさんが 作つくった ケーキ
Inside subordinate clauses, the subject takes が. は is for the main sentence topic only.

❌ Using polite form in the modifying clause

× 買かいました 本ほん → ○ 買かった 本ほん
Modifying clauses MUST use plain form, even in polite sentences. The polite ending goes only on the final verb of the main sentence.
