Grammar 1a

ひとつ〜とお — General Counter

数かずえ方かた

🎉 The Universal Counter System

Japanese uses the native counting words ひとつ, ふたつ, ... とお for counting things up to 10 when no specific counter suffix applies. For 11 and above, use the regular numbers (じゅういち, じゅうに...) without a counter. Think of this as the "safe default" — when you don't know which counter to use, these work for most objects!

Grammar 1b

Counter Suffixes

助数詞じょすうし

📖 What are Counter Suffixes?

Japanese uses different counter words depending on the shape, type, or nature of what you're counting. These suffixes attach to Sino-Japanese numbers (いち, に, さん...). Each counter is used for a specific category of objects. English has similar concepts: "two sheets of paper" or "three cups of coffee" — Japanese just uses them more broadly.

CounterUsed forExamples
〜人にんPeoplestudents, employees, children
〜台だいMachines & vehiclescars, computers, TVs
〜枚まいThin, flat thingspaper, shirts, plates, CDs
〜回かいFrequency / times"twice," "three times"
〜分ぷんMinutes5分ふん, 10分ぷん
〜時間じかんHours (duration)2時間じかん = two hours
〜日にちDays (duration)3日みっか = three days
〜週間しゅうかんWeeks2週間しゅうかん = two weeks
〜か月げつMonths (duration)3か月げつ = three months
〜年ねんYears5年ねん = five years
⚠️ Time counters are special! 時間じかん (hours) = duration (how long). 時じ (o'clock) = point in time (L4). Don't confuse them! 2時じ = "2 o'clock." 2時間じかん = "2 hours." Similarly, 1日にち for days uses the same kanji as dates but in a duration context (and 1日にち is read いちにち, not ついたち!).

Grammar 2

Quantifier Placement

数量詞すうりょうしの位置いち
📐 Structure
N を [Number + Counter] V ます

📖 How it works

Quantifiers (number + counter suffix) are placed right after the noun + particle, and directly before the verb. This position tells you "how many" of the noun the action applies to. Note that for time durations, the quantifier doesn't always follow this pattern — it can be more flexible.

📝 Example ① — Buying objects
りんごを 4つ 買かいました。
I bought four apples.
📝 Example ② — Counting people (existence)
外国人がいこくじんの 学生がくせいが 2人ふたり います。
There are two foreign students.
📝 Example ③ — Duration
国くにで 2か月げつ 日本語にほんごを 勉強べんきょうしました。
I studied Japanese for two months in my home country.
💡 Placement Rule: For object quantities: N を [Quantifier] V ます. For time durations: the quantifier can appear in various positions but usually comes before the verb. For people/things existing: N が [Quantifier] います/あります.

Grammar 3

いくつ / なん + Counter

"How many?"

📖 Two Ways to Ask "How Many?"

When asking about quantity, you use either いくつ (for generic counting) or なん + counter suffix (for specific categories). The choice depends on whether the item uses a specific counter or the general ひとつ/ふたつ system.

🔢 いくつ — General "how many?"
みかんを いくつ 買かいましたか。 ④
How many oranges did you buy?
......8つ 買かいました。
I bought eight.
🔢 なん + counter — Specific
外国人がいこくじんが 何人なんにん いますか。 ⑤
How many foreigners are there?
......5人にん います。
There are five.
💬 Example ⑥ — Duration question
A: 毎晩まいばん 何時間なんじかん 日本語にほんごを 勉強べんきょうしますか。
How many hours do you study Japanese every night?
B: ......2時間じかん 勉強べんきょうします。
I study for two hours.
💡 Quick Guide: Use いくつ for general items (apples, boxes, eggs...). Use なん人にん for people, なん枚まい for flat things, なん台だい for machines, なん時間じかん for hours, なんか月げつ for months, etc.
Grammar 4

どのくらい / ～ぐらい

Duration & "About"

📖 How it works

The interrogative どのくらい asks "how long?" or "how much?" for durations and amounts. The suffix ～ぐらい is added after quantifiers to mean "about" or "approximately." Together, they form a very practical pair for discussing time and quantities.

💬 Example ⑦ — How long?
A: どのくらい 日本語にほんごを 勉強べんきょうしましたか。
How long did you study Japanese?
B: ......3年ねん 勉強べんきょうしました。
I studied for three years.
💬 Example ⑧ — Travel duration
A: 大阪おおさかから 東京とうきょうまで どのくらい かかりますか。
How long does it take from Osaka to Tokyo?
B: ......新幹線しんかんせんで 2時間半じかんはん かかります。
It takes about two and a half hours by Shinkansen.

📖 ～ぐらい — "About"

📝 Example ⑨
学校がっこうに 先生せんせいが 30人にんぐらい います。
There are about thirty teachers at our school.
📝 Example ⑩
15分ふんぐらい かかります。
It takes about fifteen minutes.
💡 Position: ぐらい comes RIGHT AFTER the quantifier: 30人にんぐらい, 2時間じかんぐらい, 15分ふんぐらい. It softens the number from an exact count to an approximation.

Grammar 5

Period に ～回かい V

Frequency Expressions
📐 Structure
Time Period + に + [Number]回かい + V ます

📖 How it works

This pattern expresses how often you do something within a given time period. The particle に here means "per" (per week, per month, etc.), and 回かい is the counter for "times." This builds directly on the に you know from time marking (L4) — here it marks the time frame for frequency.

📝 Example ⑪
1か月げつに 2回かい 映画えいがを 見みます。
I go to see a film about twice a month.
📝 More examples
1週間しゅうかんに 3回かい ジムへ 行いきます。
I go to the gym three times a week.
1年ねんに 1回かい 国くにへ 帰かえります。
I go home once a year.
💡 Key Difference: に for frequency = "per" (1か月げつに 2回かい = 2 times PER month). This adds yet another role to the versatile particle に! Compare: 3時じに = "at 3 o'clock" (L4) vs 1週間しゅうかんに = "per week" (L11).

L4 に marks specific time: 6時じに ("at 6").

L5 に marks destination: 日本にほんに 行いきます.

L10 に marks existence location: 部屋へやに あります.

L11 に marks frequency frame: 1週間しゅうかんに 3回かい.

Grammar 6

だけ — "Only"

Limitation
📐 Structure
Quantifier + だけ
N + だけ

📖 How it works

The word だけ means "only" and is added after quantifiers or nouns to indicate that there is no more or nothing else beyond what's stated. It expresses a sense of limitation — emphasizing that the amount or item is the sole one.

📝 Example ⑫ — After quantifier
パワー電気でんきに 外国人がいこくじんの 社員しゃいんが 1人りだけ います。
There is only one foreign employee at Power Electric.
📝 Example ⑬ — After noun
休やすみは 日曜日にちようびだけです。
Sunday is my only day off.
💡 Position: だけ comes right after what it limits. After a quantifier: 1人りだけ (only one person). After a noun: 日曜日にちようびだけ (only Sunday). It always means "nothing more than this."

Duration vs Point in Time

期間きかん vs 時点じてん

⚠️ Critical Distinction

Many learners confuse time points ("at 3 o'clock," "on Monday") with time durations ("for 3 hours," "for 2 weeks"). Japanese uses different words for each!

ConceptPoint in Time (L4)Duration (L11)
Hours3時じ = "3 o'clock"3時間じかん = "3 hours"
Days3日みっか = "the 3rd" (date)3日にち = "3 days"
Months3月がつ = "March"3か月げつ = "3 months"
Years2024年ねん = "year 2024"3年ねん = "3 years"
💡 The Big Pattern: 時じ vs 時間じかん, 月がつ vs か月げつ, 日か (date) vs 日にち (duration). Points in time use に (L4), durations don't need a particle — they simply come before the verb. 3時じに 起おきます = "wake up AT 3." 3時間じかん 寝ねます = "sleep FOR 3 hours."

ぐらい vs だけ

About vs Only
📏 ぐらい — "about" (approximate)
30人にんぐらい います。
There are about 30 people.
Softens the number — "approximately"
☝️ だけ — "only" (limitation)
1人りだけ います。
There is only 1 person.
Limits the number — "nothing more"
💡 Both come after quantifiers but have opposite feelings: ぐらい = "it's roughly this much" (flexible). だけ = "it's this much and no more" (restrictive). You can even combine them! 2人りだけ = "only 2 people (exactly)." 2人りぐらい = "about 2 people."
