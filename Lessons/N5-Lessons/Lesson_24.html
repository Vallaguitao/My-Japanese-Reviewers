Grammar 1

くれます

"(Someone) gives to me / my side"

📖 Why do we need くれます?

In L7, you learned あげます for giving things. However, あげます cannot be used when someone gives to you (the speaker) or a member of your family/in-group. In that case, Japanese uses くれます. This reflects a deep cultural principle: Japanese distinguishes between giving TO others vs giving TO me, because acknowledging what others do for you expresses gratitude.

📝 Example ① — あげます: I give TO others
わたしは 佐藤さとうさんに 花はなを あげました。
I gave some flowers to Ms. Sato.
❌ × 佐藤さとうさんは わたしに クリスマスカードを あげました。

You CANNOT use あげます when someone gives to YOU. あげます = outward giving only!

📝 Example ② — くれます: Others give TO ME
佐藤さとうさんは わたしに クリスマスカードを くれました。
Ms. Sato gave me a Christmas card.
📝 Example ③ — くれます: Others give to MY FAMILY
佐藤さとうさんは 妹いもうとに お菓子かしを くれました。
Ms. Sato gave some candies to my younger sister.

Grammar 2

Things → Actions

もの → 行為こうい

📖 From giving THINGS to giving ACTIONS

In L7, you learned あげます/もらいます/くれます for giving and receiving things. L24 extends the same logic to actions — doing something as a favor for someone. The て-form of the action verb connects to あげます/もらいます/くれます. The direction rules are identical: the only difference is that instead of giving an object, you're giving the benefit of an action.

📐 Structure — Actions as Favors
V て-form + あげます — I do ~ for someone
V て-form + もらいます — Someone does ~ for me (I receive)
V て-form + くれます — Someone does ~ for me (they give)
💡 Same three verbs, same directions! あげます = outward (I → other), もらいます = I receive (other → me), くれます = they give to me (other → me). The ONLY new thing is adding て-form before them to express actions instead of things. If you understood L7's giving/receiving, L24 is a natural extension!

L7 N を あげます — Give a thing TO someone

L7 N を もらいます — Receive a thing FROM someone

L24 くれます — Someone gives a thing TO me/my side (new!)

L24 V て あげます — Do an action FOR someone

L24 V て もらいます — Have someone do an action for me

L24 V て くれます — Someone does an action for me

L7 = things. L24 = actions. Same giving/receiving logic, now applied to favors!

Grammar 2a

V て あげます

"I do ~ for (someone)"

📖 Doing a favor for someone else

The doer of the action is the subject, and the action benefits the recipient (marked by に). It shows that the action confers benefit or profit on the other person. Be careful: because てあげます implies conferring a benefit, it can sound patronizing if the recipient is of higher status. In those cases, use ましょうか (L14) instead!

📝 Example ④
わたしは 木村きむらさんに 本ほんを 貸かして あげました。
I lent Ms. Kimura a book. (= I did her a favor)
わたし (doer)
→ て あげます →
action benefits
木村きむらさん (に)
⚠️ てあげます can sound patronizing!

For higher-status people (boss, teacher, senior), DON'T say × 先生せんせいに 教おしえてあげます (sounds like you're doing them a favor). Instead use ましょうか (L14):

📝 Examples ⑤⑥ — Use ましょうか instead
タクシーを 呼よびましょうか。 (Shall I call a cab?)
手伝てつだいましょうか。 (May I help you?)
Grammar 2b

V て もらいます

"(Someone) does ~ for me" — I receive the action

📖 Receiving a favor from someone

The speaker (or speaker's side) is the subject — the one who receives the benefit. The person who does the action is marked by に. This pattern expresses gratitude by emphasizing that YOU received the benefit. When the subject is わたし, it's usually omitted.

📝 Example ⑦
[わたしは] 山田やまださんに 図書館としょかんの 電話番号でんわばんごうを 教おしえて もらいました。
Mr. Yamada told me the telephone number of the library. (= I received the favor)
わたし (receiver)
← て もらいます ←
I receive the benefit
山田やまださん (に)
💡 てもらいます vs てくれます — both mean "someone does for me" but differ in perspective! てもらいます = I receive (focus on MY receiving). てくれます = they give (focus on THEIR giving). The next slide explains てくれます!
Grammar 2c

V て くれます

"(Someone) does ~ for me" — They give the action

📖 Someone does something for my benefit

The person who does the action is the subject. The speaker (or speaker's side) is the beneficiary, marked by に (often omitted when it's わたし). This pattern shows the speaker feels the other person's action was a kind act that benefited them. It's the action equivalent of くれます (giving things to me).

📝 Example ⑧
母ははは [わたしに] セーターを 送おくって くれました。
My mother sent me a sweater. (= she kindly did it for me)
母はは (doer)
→ て くれます →
they kindly give action
わたし (beneficiary)

📖 More てくれます examples from the textbook (p. 153)

The textbook shows how regular sentences become てくれます sentences by adding the gratitude element. The particles stay the same — only the verb ending changes to てくれます:

Neutral statement
旅行りょこうの 写真しゃしんを 見みせます。
He shows travel photos.
+ くれます (gratitude!)
旅行りょこうの 写真しゃしんを 見みせて くれます。
He (kindly) shows me the travel photos.
Neutral statement
引ひっ越こしを 手伝てつだいます。

+ くれます (gratitude!)
引ひっ越こしを 手伝てつだって くれます。
He (kindly) helps me move.

Compare

てもらいます vs てくれます

違ちがいを理解りかいする

📖 Both mean "someone does for me" — so what's the difference?

The key difference is who is the grammatical subject and whose perspective the sentence takes.

🟢 てもらいます — MY perspective
[わたしは] 山田やまださんに 教おしえて もらいました。
I had Mr. Yamada teach me.

Subject = わたし (receiver). Focus: "I received this benefit."
Nuance: I actively sought or appreciate the help.

🔴 てくれます — THEIR perspective
山田やまださんが 教おしえて くれました。
Mr. Yamada taught me (kindly).

Subject = 山田さん (doer). Focus: "They were kind enough to do this."
Nuance: Emphasizes the other person's kindness.

💡 Quick rule of thumb: てもらいます = "I had someone do ~" (I'm the subject, I received). てくれます = "Someone kindly did ~ for me" (they're the subject, they gave). Both express gratitude, but from different angles. In everyday Japanese, てくれます is extremely common and adds warmth to your speech!

Grammar 3

N₁ は N₂ が V

Topicalized Object

📖 When the topic IS the object

Sometimes the object of a sentence becomes the topic (は), and the subject is marked by が. This happens when both speaker and listener already know what they're talking about (the object is shared context), so it becomes the topic. The person who did the action is then marked by が.

📝 Example ⑨
おいしい ワインですね。
This wine is very nice, isn't it?
……ええ。[この ワインは] 佐藤さとうさんが くれました。
...Yes. Ms. Sato gave it to me.

The full sentence would be: 佐藤さとうさんが この ワインを くれました. But since "this wine" is already the topic of conversation, it becomes は (or is omitted entirely). 佐藤さとうさん, now needing a particle, takes が as the subject/agent.

💡 This is the L17-5 pattern! When the object is topicalized (moved to は position), the subject takes が. You've seen similar patterns: N は N₂ が 好すき (L9), N は N₂ が あります (L10). The principle is: when は is already used for the topic, the subject/agent uses が.

L9 わたしは 音楽おんがくが 好すきです — Topic は + what you like が

L10 わたしは 車くるまが あります — Topic は + what you have が

L17 Topic は ... が — Topicalized object pattern

L22 Clause subject → が (inside modifying clauses)

L24 [ワインは] 佐藤さとうさんが くれました — Object topicalized, agent が

💬 Talking About Helpful Friends
A: 引ひっ越こし、大変たいへんでしたか。
Was the move hard?
B: いいえ、田中たなかさんが 手伝てつだって くれました。

Culture

Why This Grammar Matters

文化的ぶんかてき背景はいけい

🌸 The heart of Japanese social awareness

English speakers often wonder why Japanese needs three verbs where English uses one ("give/do for"). The answer lies in Japanese culture's emphasis on awareness of social relationships and gratitude. By choosing あげます/もらいます/くれます, the speaker communicates not just what happened, but how they feel about the social exchange — who benefited, who was kind, and from whose perspective the story is told.

💡 In practice: てくれます is extremely common in daily Japanese — it's how Japanese speakers express warmth and appreciation. Saying 母が 作ってくれた instead of just 母が 作った adds a layer of "and I'm grateful for it." Mastering this grammar is key to sounding natural and emotionally connected in Japanese!
🎯 Frequency in real Japanese: てくれます is arguably the MOST culturally important grammar pattern in N5. Native speakers use it constantly — "友達が 来てくれた" (my friend came [and I appreciate it]), "先生が 教えてくれた" (the teacher taught me [and I'm thankful]). It adds emotional color to every sentence!

Caution

Common Mistakes

よくある間違まちがい
❌ Using あげます for giving TO me

× 友達ともだちは わたしに プレゼントを あげました → ○ … くれました
あげます can NEVER be used when someone gives to the speaker or speaker's in-group. Always use くれます for inward giving!

❌ Confusing に with もらいます vs あげます

てあげます: 田中たなかさんに = recipient (I do for Tanaka)
てもらいます: 田中たなかさんに = doer (Tanaka does for me)
Same particle に, different roles! Check which verb follows.

❌ Using てあげます with higher-status people

× 先生せんせいに 教おしえてあげます (patronizing!) → ○ 先生せんせいに お教おしえします / ましょうか
てあげます implies "I'm doing YOU a favor" — rude to superiors. Use ましょうか (L14) or humble forms.

Key Rule

に = Different Roles!

「に」の二重にじゅう役割やくわり
Patternに marksExample
