Grammar 1

Verb Groups

動詞どうしのグループ

📖 Why Verb Groups Matter

Japanese verbs are classified into three groups based on how they conjugate. Until now, you've only used the ます-form, which hides the group differences. Starting with the て-form, the group a verb belongs to determines how it changes. Learning which group a verb belongs to is essential from this point forward.

🔴 Group I
ます-form ends in い-column sound
書かきます
飲のみます
買かいます
待まちます
🔵 Group II
ます-form ends in え-column (mostly)
食たべます
見みます ⚠️
みせます
起おきます ⚠️
🟣 Group III
Irregular: only 2 verbs + する compounds
します
来きます
散歩さんぽします
勉強べんきょうします
💡 Quick identification: If ます-form ends in え-row → Group II. If します or 来きます → Group III. Everything else → Group I. Some verbs like 見みます and 起おきます look like Group I but are actually Group II — memorize these exceptions!
Grammar 2a

🔴 Group I て-form

て-form のつくり方かた

📖 The Core Challenge

Group I is the trickiest because the て-form ending changes depending on the last sound of the ます-form. There are four sub-rules to learn. The ます-form's last sound determines what replaces it before て/で.

Rule
ます-form → て-form
Examples
(1) い、ち、り → って
買かいます → 買かって
→
待まちます → 待まって
帰かえります → 帰かえって
(2) み、び、に → んで
飲のみます → 飲のんで
→
呼よびます → 呼よんで
死しにます → 死しんで
(3) き → いて / ぎ → いで
書かきます → 書かいて
→
急いそぎます → 急いそいで
(4) し → して
貸かします → 貸かして
→
話はなします → 話はなして
⚠️ Exception! 行いきます (to go) is Group I rule (1), but instead of いいて, it becomes 行いって. This is the ONLY irregular Group I verb!
Memory Aid

て-form Mnemonic Song

覚おぼえ方かた

🎵 The て-form Song (Group I)

い ち り → って
み び に → んで
き → いて   ぎ → いで
し → して
Say it rhythmically: "i-chi-ri TTE, mi-bi-ni NDE, ki ITE, gi IDE, shi SHITE!"
Repeat this pattern 10 times and it will stick forever!
💡 Practice strategy: Write out every Group I verb you know, extract the ます-form's last sound, and apply the matching rule. Start with common verbs: 買かいます(い→って), 飲のみます(み→んで), 書かきます(き→いて), 話はなします(し→して).
Grammar 2b

🔵 Group II & 🟣 III て-form

グループ II・III
🔵 Group II — Simple!

Just add て to the ます-form:

食たべます → 食たべて

見みます → 見みて

みせます → みせて

🟣 Group III — Irregular

Memorize these two:

します → して

来きます → 来きて

散歩さんぽします → 散歩さんぽして

💡 Group II is your friend! All Group II verbs follow the same simple rule: ます-form + て. No sound changes, no exceptions. Group III has only two base verbs (します, 来きます) — everything else is a する compound. The real challenge is Group I's four sub-rules!

Grammar 3

V て ください

"Please do ~"
📐 Structure
V て-form + ください

📖 How it works

This is the most practical て-form pattern — making requests. You can use it to ask someone to do something (requesting), to tell someone to do something (telling), or to invite someone (inviting). It's often combined with すみませんが for politeness. Note: while useful, it's not the most polite request form — it's direct and best used with people you're comfortable with or in standard situations.

📝 Example ① — Requesting
すみませんが、この 漢字かんじの 読よみ方かたを 教おしえて ください。
Excuse me, could you tell me how to read this kanji, please?
📝 Example ② — Telling
ボールペンで 名前なまえを 書かいて ください。
Please write your name with a ballpoint pen.
📝 Example ③ — Inviting
どうぞ たくさん 食たべて ください。
Please eat as much as you like.

L3 N を ください — "Give me N, please" (objects only)

L6 V ませんか — "Won't you ~?" (invitation)

L14 V て ください — "Please do ~" (requests/instructions)

L17 V ないで ください — "Please DON'T do ~" (negative requests)

L25 V て-form も いいですか — "May I ~?" (permission)

Grammar 4

V て います

Ongoing Action / State
📐 Structure
V て-form + います

📖 How it works

This pattern indicates that a certain action or motion is in progress. Think of it like English "-ing" forms ("is eating," "is raining"). It captures an action happening right now or a state that is currently true. The negative is て いません.

📝 Example ④ — Action in progress
ミラーさんは 今いま 電話でんわを かけて います。
Mr. Miller is making a phone call right now.
💬 Example ⑤ — Weather
A: 今いま 雨あめが 降ふって いますか。
Is it raining now?
B: はい、降ふって います。
Yes, it is [raining].
💡 ています has multiple uses! In L14, it means "is [currently] doing" (ongoing action). In later lessons, it will also express: habitual actions (L15: "I teach Japanese" = 日本語にほんごを 教おしえています), resulting states (L15: "結婚けっこんしています" = "is married"), and more. This is just the beginning!

Grammar 5

V ます-form ましょうか

"Shall I ~?"
📐 Structure
V ます-form + ましょうか

📖 How it works

This pattern is used when the speaker is offering to do something for the listener. It's a considerate way to volunteer help. You already learned ましょう (let's ~) in L6 — adding か turns "let's do" into "shall I do [for you]?" A subtle but important shift from joint action to personal offer.

💬 Example ⑥ — Shall I come?
A: あしたも 来きましょうか。
Shall I come tomorrow, too?
B: ええ、10時じに 来きて ください。
Yes, please come at ten.
💬 Example ⑦ — Shall I lend?
A: 傘かさを 貸かしましょうか。
Shall I lend you my umbrella?
B: すみません。お願ねがいします。
Sorry [to trouble you]. Please.
💬 Example ⑧ — Shall I carry? (declining)
A: 荷物にもつを 持もちましょうか。
Shall I carry your bags?
B: いいえ、けっこうです。
No, thank you.
💡 Responding to offers: Accept: すみません。お願ねがいします。 (Please.) / ええ、〜て ください。 Decline: いいえ、けっこうです。 (No, thank you.) / 大丈夫だいじょうぶです。 (I'm fine.)
Grammar 6 & 7

N が V / すみませんが

Phenomena & Preamble

📖 Grammar 6: N が V — Sensory / Phenomenon

When describing a natural phenomenon perceived through the senses (sight, hearing, etc.) or reporting an event objectively, the subject is marked with が. This builds on L10's existence が (N が あります/います) — here it extends to describing what you observe happening in the world around you.

📝 Example ⑨ — Natural phenomenon
雨あめが 降ふって います。
It's raining.
📝 Example ⑩ — Observation
ミラーさんが いませんね。
Mr. Miller isn't here, is he?

📖 Grammar 7: すみませんが — Polite Preamble

The が in すみませんが and 失礼しつれいですが functions as a soft transition ("but..."), not as a contrasting conjunction. It's used as a polite lead-in before making a request or asking a question — similar to English "Excuse me, but..."

📝 Example ⑪ — Polite request
すみませんが、塩しおを 取とって ください。
Excuse me, could you pass the salt, please?
📝 Example ⑫ — Polite inquiry
失礼しつれいですが、お名前なまえは？
Excuse me, but what is your name?
💡 Note: This が is NOT the contrasting "but" from L8. It's a softer, introductory "but" that functions as a politeness device — cushioning a request so it doesn't sound too abrupt.
