Grammar 1

これ / それ / あれ

Demonstrative Pronouns

📖 How it works

これ, それ, and あれ are demonstrative pronouns — they stand in for a noun and point to things. Japanese uses a three-way system based on the distance between the speaker, the listener, and the object.

Unlike English (which has just "this" and "that"), Japanese distinguishes between something near the listener (それ) and something far from both people (あれ). This system is called the こ-そ-あ (ko-so-a) system and is one of the most important patterns in the language.

👆 Distance Diagram — Ko-So-A System
🙋‍♂️📦 これ this (thing) Near the speaker
🙋‍♂️ ↔ 📦🙋‍♀️ それ that (thing) Near the listener
🙋‍♂️ 🙋‍♀️ ↔ ↔ 📦 あれ that over there Far from both
📝 Example ①
それは 辞書じしょですか。
Is that a dictionary? (object near the listener)
📝 Example ②
これは だれの 傘かさですか。
Whose umbrella is this? (object near the speaker)
💡 Key Point: これ/それ/あれ work as complete nouns — they replace the name of the thing entirely. You can say これは ～です just like わたしは ～です from Lesson 1!

Building on Lesson 1 & Looking Ahead:

L1 You already know N₁ は N₂ です. Now これ/それ/あれ slot into N₁'s position perfectly!

L3 The ko-so-a system expands to places: ここ/そこ/あそこ ("here/there/over there").

L14 You'll meet これ/それ again with て-form: これを 見みてください ("Please look at this").

L16 Object descriptions expand: これは 新あたらしくて 便利べんりです ("This is new and convenient").

Grammar 2

この N / その N / あの N

Demonstrative Modifiers
📐 Structure
この / その / あの + Noun

📖 How it works

While これ/それ/あれ stand alone as pronouns, この/その/あの must always be followed by a noun — they modify the noun that comes after them. Think of them as "this ~", "that ~", and "that ~ over there."

The distance rules are exactly the same as for これ/それ/あれ: この (near speaker), その (near listener), あの (far from both).

🔵 この + N
この 本ほん
this book
Near speaker
🟢 その + N
その かばん
that bag
Near listener
🩷 あの + N
あの 方かた
that person (over there)
Far from both
📝 Example ③
この 本ほんは わたしのです。
This book is mine.
📝 Example ④
あの 方かたは どなたですか。
Who is that person (over there)?
📌 Pronoun (stands alone)
これは 本ほんです。
This is a book.
📌 Modifier (+ noun)
この 本ほんは わたしのです。
This book is mine.
💡 Key Point: Never use この/その/あの without a noun after it!
✅ この本ほん → ❌ この は 本ほんです (wrong — use これ instead)

The ko-so-a pattern repeats everywhere:

L1 あの 方かたは どなたですか — you already saw this pattern!

L3 Place modifiers: この/その/あの 部屋へや ("this/that room").

L8 With adjectives: この 映画えいがは おもしろいです ("This movie is interesting").

L15 Expanded descriptions: あの 背せが 高たかい 人ひと ("That tall person over there").

Grammar 3

そうです / ちがいます

Confirming & Denying

📖 How it works

When answering a yes/no question about an object (like "Is that a dictionary?"), you can confirm with はい、そうです ("Yes, that's right") or deny with いいえ、ちがいます ("No, that's wrong/different").

Notice that the negative answer does NOT usually use そう — instead, Japanese speakers say ちがいます (literally "it differs/it's wrong") and then give the correct answer.

💬 Example ⑤ — Affirmative (Yes)
A: それは 辞書じしょですか。
Is that a dictionary?
B: はい、そうです。
Yes, it is. (lit. "Yes, that's right.")
💬 Example ⑥ — Negative (Correcting)
A: それは ミラーさんのですか。
Is that Mr. Miller's?
B: いいえ、ちがいます。
No, it isn't. (lit. "No, it's different.")
💬 Example ⑦ — Correcting with the right answer
A: それは シャープペンシルですか。
Is that a mechanical pencil?
B: いいえ、ボールペンです。
No, it's a ballpoint pen.
✅ Confirm
はい、そうです。
Yes, that's right.
❌ Deny
いいえ、ちがいます。
No, that's wrong.
💡 Key Point: そう is NOT usually used in the negative. Don't say "そうじゃ ありません" — say ちがいます instead, then state the correct answer.

Confirming and denying patterns grow:

L1 はい/いいえ for simple yes/no answers — そうです adds a more natural confirmation layer.

L8 Adjective confirmation: 「おいしいですか。」「はい、とても おいしいです。」

L12 Past confirmation: 「昨日きのうは 暇ひまでしたか。」「はい、そうです。」

L25 Conditional: ～たら、そうします ("If so, I'll do that").

Grammar 4

〜か、〜か

Alternative Questions
📐 Structure
N₁ ですか、N₂ ですか。

📖 How it works

This pattern asks the listener to choose between two (or more) alternatives — like saying "Is it A or B?" in English. Each alternative ends with ですか, and they're separated by a comma (、) in writing and a brief pause in speech.

When answering, you simply state the correct choice directly — you do not say はい or いいえ. Just give the answer.

💬 Example ⑧
A: これは「9」ですか、「7」ですか。
Is this a 9 or a 7?
B: 「9」です。
It's a 9.
💡 Key Point: When answering alternative questions (～か、～か), do not begin with はい or いいえ. Just state the chosen alternative directly.

Alternative patterns reappear:

L4 Time alternatives: 今日きょうですか、明日あしたですか ("Is it today or tomorrow?").

L8 Adjective alternatives: 大おおきいですか、小ちいさいですか ("Is it big or small?").

L14 Verb alternatives: 行いきますか、行いきませんか ("Will you go or not?").

Grammar 5

N₁ の N₂ — Expanded

の の 新あたらしい 使つかい方かた

📖 Review from Lesson 1

In Lesson 1, you learned that の connects two nouns, where N₁ modifies N₂ (mainly for affiliation: IMCの 社員しゃいん). Now in Lesson 2, の expands to cover two more important uses: what something is about and who owns something.

🔹 Use 1: N₁ explains what N₂ is about

N₁ tells you the subject or topic of N₂.

📝 Example ⑨
これは コンピューターの 本ほんです。
This is a book on (about) computers.

🔹 Use 2: N₁ indicates who owns N₂

N₁ is the owner, and N₂ is what they own.

📝 Example ⑩
これは わたしの 本ほんです。
This is my book.
Use N₁ の N₂ Meaning Lesson
Affiliation IMCの 社員しゃいん IMC's employee L1
Topic/About コンピューターの 本ほん a book about computers L2
Ownership わたしの 本ほん my book L2
💡 Key Point: The particle の is incredibly versatile! The relationship between N₁ and N₂ depends on context. With practice, you'll read the meaning naturally. For now, remember these three uses: affiliation, topic, and ownership.

の keeps growing:

L1 Affiliation: IMCの 社員しゃいん — the first use you learned.

L3 Location: 日本語にほんごの 教室きょうしつ ("Japanese language classroom").

L5 Origin: 日本にほんの おみやげ ("souvenir from Japan").

L8 Material/Type: 木きの テーブル ("wooden table").

Grammar 6

の Substituting for a Noun

の（名詞めいしの代かわり）

📖 How it works

The particle の can replace a noun that has already been mentioned, to avoid repeating it. Think of it like saying "one" or "mine" in English — it stands in for a noun the listener already knows from context.

This only works for things, not for people.

📐 Structure
N₁ の N₂ → N₁ の　N₂
💬 Example ⑪ — "Whose bag?"
A: あれは だれの かばんですか。
Whose bag is that?
B: 佐藤さとうさんのです。
It's Ms. Sato's. (= 佐藤さとうさんの かばん です)
💬 Example ⑫ — "Is this bag yours?"
A: この かばんは あなたのですか。
Is this bag yours?
B: いいえ、わたしのじゃ ありません。
No, it isn't mine. (= わたしの かばん じゃ ありません)
💬 Example ⑬ — Company affiliation
A: ミラーさんは IMCの 社員しゃいんですか。
Does Mr. Miller work at IMC?
B: はい、IMCのです。
Yes, he does. (= IMCの 社員しゃいん です)
⚠️ Important: Dropping the noun with の only works for things and roles, never for people. Don't say あのの は だれですか — instead say あの 方かたは.
✅ Correct — replaces a thing
これは 佐藤さとうさんのです。
(= 佐藤さとうさんの かばん)
❌ Wrong — cannot replace a person
× はい、IMCの です。(meaning "person")
Only use for things, not people!

Grammar 7

お〜 Polite Prefix

お + 言葉ことば

📖 How it works

The prefix お is attached to certain words to make them sound more polite and refined. This is common in everyday Japanese and reflects the language's emphasis on showing respect to others.

You'll see お attached to many common words, especially those related to things you give to or share with others.

Base Word With お Meaning
みやげ おみやげ souvenir / gift
さけ おさけ sake / alcohol
ちゃ おちゃ tea
金かね お金かね money
名前なまえ お名前なまえ name (polite)
💡 Key Point: Not every word can take お — it's mainly attached to native Japanese words (和語わご). Words borrowed from other languages (like コーヒー or テレビ) do not usually take お. You'll naturally learn which words use it through practice!

Politeness markers appear throughout:

L1 さん — the first honorific you learned. お adds another layer of politeness.

L3 お国くに ("your country") — polite way to ask someone's country of origin.

L6 お花見はなみ ("flower viewing") — cultural word with お prefix.

L14 お願ねがいします ("Please") — one of the most common polite expressions.

L25 お + verb stem = respectful form: お待まちください ("Please wait").

Grammar 8

そうですか

Acknowledging Information

📖 How it works

そうですか (with a falling intonation ↓) is an expression used to acknowledge new information you've just received. It's like saying "I see," "Oh really," or "Is that so?" in English.

Be careful not to confuse this with そうですか↑ (rising intonation) which means "Is that so?" as a genuine question. The falling intonation version simply shows you've understood what was said.

💬 Example ⑭
A: この 傘かさは あなたのですか。
Is this umbrella yours?
B: いいえ、ちがいます。シュミットさんのです。
No, it isn't. It's Mr. Schmidt's.
A: そうですか。
Oh, I see. ↓
⬇️ Falling intonation
そうですか。↓
"I see." / "Oh, really."
Acknowledging info you just learned
⬆️ Rising intonation
そうですか？↑
"Is that so?" / "Really?"
Genuine question — expressing surprise
💡 Key Point: そうですか↓ is one of the most useful "listener responses" (相槌あいづち) in Japanese. Saying it naturally shows you're engaged and have understood. You'll hear it constantly in real conversations!
