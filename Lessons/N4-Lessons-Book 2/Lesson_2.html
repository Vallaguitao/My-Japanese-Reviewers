Grammar 1

N1 は N2 が ナA / イA

Describing Physical Features

🔍 What does it mean?

This pattern is used to describe a person's external, physical characteristics. N1 is the person, and N2 is a body part. The adjective describes that body part. Think of it as: "As for (person), their (body part) is (description)."

📐 Formation
N1 (person) は N2 (body part) が { ナA-です / イA-いです }
🧑 Body Part (N2) Description Full Example
背せ (height) 高たかい / 低ひくい 田中さんは背せが高たかいです。(Tanaka-san is tall.)
髪かみ (hair) 長ながい / 短みじかい 江口えぐちさんは髪かみが短みじかいです。(Eguchi-san has short hair.)
手て (hands) 大おおきい / 小ちいさい あの人ひとは手てが大おおきいです。(That person has large hands.)
目め (eyes) 黒くろい / 大おおきい リサさんは目めが黒くろいです。(Lisa has dark brown eyes.)
顔かお (face) 丸まるい あの子こは顔かおが丸まるいです。(That child has a round face.)
📝 Example 1
加藤かとうさんは、いちばん背せが高たかいです。
Kato-san is the tallest.
📝 Example 2 — Qualifying Nouns
江口えぐちさんは、髪かみが短みじかい人ひとです。
Eguchi-san is the person with short hair.
💡 Key Insight: Notice the particle が marks the body part, not を or は. This is because the body part is the subject of the description within the larger topic (the person). You can also turn this into a noun modifier: 髪かみが短みじかい人ひと = "a person whose hair is short" = "the person with short hair."

MNN L9 N1 は N2 が ～です (characteristics)

In MNN Lesson 9, you first learned this double-subject pattern for describing characteristics of topics: 日本語にほんごは文法ぶんぽうが難むずかしいです ("As for Japanese, the grammar is difficult"). This lesson applies the same structure specifically to describing people's physical features — N1 is always a person and N2 is always a body part.

MNN L8 イ-adjective / ナ-adjective basics

The adjective types from MNN Lesson 8 (おおきい vs. しずかな) are used here to describe body parts. Remember: イA-いです (高いです) and ナA-です (丸いです is actually イA!).

Grammar 2

V-ている人ひと

"The Person Who Is V-ing"

🔍 What does it mean?

This expression is used to identify or describe a person by what they are currently doing or what state they are in. Instead of using the polite form V-ています, you use the plain form V-ている to modify the noun 人ひと directly. It's like creating a mini relative clause: "the person who is wearing / doing / reading ~."

📐 Formation
V-ている + 人ひと

The plain form V-ている (not V-ています) is used because it directly modifies the noun 人.

👔 Two Types of V-ている

Remember from 初級しょきゅう1 that V-ている has two main uses. Both work with this pattern:

👕 ① Resulting State

The person is wearing / has on something (result of putting it on).

帽子ぼうしをかぶっている人ひと

the person wearing a hat

📖 ② Ongoing Action

The person is currently doing something.

コピーをしている人ひと

the person making copies

👗 Clothing & Accessory Verbs

Japanese uses different verbs depending on where on the body you wear something:

Verb Used For Example
～を着きている Clothing (upper body, full body) シャツを着きている人ひと 👔
～をはいている Lower body (pants, shoes, socks) ジーンズをはいている人ひと 👖
～をかぶっている Head (hats, caps) 帽子ぼうしをかぶっている人ひと 🧢
～をつけている Accessories (watches, earrings) 時計とけいをつけている人ひと ⌚
～をかけている Glasses, sunglasses メガネをかけている人ひと 👓
💬 Dialogue — Identifying Someone
A：江口えぐちさんはどの人ひとですか？
Which person is Eguchi-san?
B：あの髪かみが短みじかくて、ひげをはやしてる人ひとですよ。
That person with short hair and a beard.
📝 More Examples
ムックさんは、あそこで先生せんせいと話はなしている人ひとです。
Mook-san is the person talking with the teacher over there.
💡 Key Insight: You can combine Grammar 1 and Grammar 2 to give rich descriptions: 髪かみが短みじかくて、メガネをかけている人ひと ("the person with short hair who is wearing glasses"). This uses the て-form to chain descriptions — just like ～て、～ from Lesson 1!

MNN L14 V-ています (present state / progressive)

In MNN Lesson 14, you learned V-ています for current states: 田中たなかさんは結婚けっこんしています ("Tanaka-san is married"). This lesson takes V-ている (the plain form) and uses it to modify 人ひと, creating descriptions like 帽子ぼうしをかぶっている人ひと ("the person wearing a hat").

MNN L22 Verb (plain form) + Noun (noun modification)

MNN Lesson 22 introduced modifying nouns with plain-form verbs: 私わたしが撮とった写真しゃしん ("the photo I took"). V-ている人ひと is the same structure but with the progressive form: "the person who IS doing ~."

Grammar 3

S よ

Giving New Information / Alerting

🔍 What does it mean?

The sentence-ending particle よ is added when you want to communicate information that is new to the listener, or when you want to alert or inform someone of something they don't know yet. In this lesson, it's used specifically when helping someone identify a person they're looking for.

📐 Formation
Sentence (plain or polite) + よ

Simply add よ to the end of any sentence to signal "I'm telling you something you didn't know."

📌 Two Main Uses of よ

💬 Providing New Info

原はらさんは、座すわって、本ほんを読よんでる人ひとですよ。

Hara-san is the person sitting down and reading a book, you know.

⚠️ Warning / Alerting

危あぶないですよ！

It's dangerous! (Watch out!)

車くるまが来きますよ。

A car is coming! (Heads up!)

💬 Dialogue — Finding Someone
A：すみません。田中たなかさんは、どの人ひとですか。
Excuse me. Which person is Tanaka-san?
B：あそこに座すわってる人ひとですよ。
It's the person sitting over there. (new info for you!)
A：あの人ひとですね。ありがとうございます。
That person, right? (confirming) Thank you.
💡 よ vs. ね: Don't confuse these two particles! よ = "I'm telling you something new" (speaker → listener). ね = "Right? You agree?" (seeking confirmation). In the dialogue above, B uses よ to provide info, and A uses ね to confirm it. They work as a natural pair!

MNN L4 ～ね (confirmation particle)

In MNN Lesson 4 (and also 入門にゅうもん), you learned ね for confirming shared information: いい天気てんきですね ("Nice weather, isn't it?"). よ is the complement of ね — instead of seeking agreement, よ asserts new information. Together they create natural conversation flow: B provides info (よ) → A confirms (ね).

MNN L17 V-ないでください (negative requests)

The warning use of よ (危あぶないですよ) relates to the safety language from MNN L17. You can combine them: 触さわらないでください。危あぶないですよ ("Please don't touch it. It's dangerous!").

Grammar 4

S し、～

Listing Reasons & Grounds

🔍 What does it mean?

The particle し is used to list multiple reasons, grounds, or qualities that support an opinion or impression. It's like saying "and also" or "not only... but also..." when giving reasons. Unlike ～て which neutrally lists, し carries the nuance of building a case for an opinion.

📐 Formation
Reason 1 し、Reason 2 し、Conclusion/Opinion

Connects to both polite form (～ですし、～ますし) and plain form (～だし、～いし). This lesson focuses on the plain form with adjectives.

📐 Plain Form Conjugation for し

Type Polite Form + し (Plain) Example
イ-adj かっこいいです かっこいいし かっこいいし、歌うたも上手じょうずだし
ナ-adj 上手じょうずです 上手じょうずだし 演技えんぎが上手じょうずだし
Noun + だ いい人ひとです いい人ひとだし いい人ひとだし
Verb 歌うたいます 歌うたうし 歌うたもうまいし
🌟 Key Example
私わたしは安室あむろ奈美恵なみえが好すきです。かっこいいし、歌うたもダンスも上手じょうずだし。
I like Amuro Namie. She's cool, and she's good at both singing and dancing.
📝 Example — Describing a Person
三船みふね敏郎としろうは、演技えんぎが上手じょうずだし、本当ほんとうにかっこいいです。
Mifune Toshiro is good at acting, and truly cool.
💡 Key Insight: し implies there are more reasons you could give, even if you only list one or two. It creates a feeling of "among other things..." or "for one thing..." This is different from ～て (neutral listing) or ～から (single cause-effect). Use し when you want to build a positive (or negative) impression with multiple supporting points.

MNN L9 ～から (giving a reason)

MNN Lesson 9 introduced ～から for giving a single reason: おもしろいですから、好すきです ("Because it's interesting, I like it"). ～し is the upgrade: it lets you list multiple reasons at once, giving a richer explanation for your opinion. Compare: おもしろいから好きです (one reason) vs. おもしろいし、かっこいいし、好きです (multiple reasons).

MNN L20 Plain form (普通形)

The plain form conjugations you practiced in MNN Lesson 20 are essential here. し attaches to the plain form: いい (not いいです), 上手だ (not 上手です), 歌う (not 歌います). Make sure you're comfortable converting between polite and plain forms.

Bonus

よ vs. ね — Quick Reference

終助詞しゅうじょしの比較ひかく

🔑 Understanding the Difference

These two particles are the most common sentence-enders in Japanese conversation. Understanding when to use each is crucial for natural-sounding speech.

📢 よ — "I'm telling you!"

Speaker knows, listener doesn't.

あの人ひとですよ。

"It's that person." (new info)

危あぶないですよ。

"It's dangerous!" (alert)

🤝 ね — "Right? You agree?"

Both speaker and listener know.

あの人ひとですね。

"That person, right?" (confirming)

いい天気てんきですね。

"Nice weather, isn't it?" (shared)

💡 Pro Tip: In real conversation, よ and ね often appear together as よね. This softens the assertion: あの映画えいが、おもしろかったよね ("That movie was fun, wasn't it?"). It means "I think so, and I believe you agree." It's one of the most natural-sounding endings in casual Japanese.
