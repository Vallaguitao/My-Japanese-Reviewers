Grammar 1

N1 という N2

"A thing called N1"

🔍 What does it mean?

This expression is used to introduce or explain something to someone who you think does not know about it, or who is hearing about it for the first time. Think of it as the Japanese way of saying "called," "named," or "which means."

📐 Formation
N1 という N2

N1 = the name or word being introduced  |  N2 = the category (person, place, thing, dish, etc.)

Pattern Meaning Example
～という意味いみ means ~ ホアは「花はな」という意味いみです。
～という料理りょうり a dish called ~ フォーという料理りょうりです。
～という町まち a town called ~ ダナンという町まちから来きました。
～という店みせ a store called ~ 「さくら」という店みせで働はたらいています。
～という人ひと a person called ~ タンさんという人ひとです。
📝 Example 1
ベトナムのダナンという町まちから来きました。
I came from a city called Da Nang in Vietnam.
📝 Example 2
今いまは、「東京とうきょうイン」というホテルで働はたらいています。
Now, I work at a hotel called Tokyo Inn.
💡 Key Insight: Use ～という when you assume the listener doesn't know the name you're mentioning. If they already know it, you can just say the name directly without という. For example, if everyone knows "Tokyo Tower," you don't need という — but if you're introducing a small local restaurant, という helps the listener understand you're explaining something new.

MNN L3 これ / それ / あれは ～です

In MNN Lesson 3, you learned to identify objects with これは～です ("This is ~"). The という pattern builds on that by letting you name something specifically: instead of just これは料理りょうりです ("This is a dish"), you can now say これはフォーという料理りょうりです ("This is a dish called pho").

MNN L1 ～は ～です (Introduction)

In Lesson 1, you introduced yourself with わたしは～です. The という pattern extends introductions to explain the meaning of names: ホアは「花はな」という意味いみです ("Hoa means 'flower'").

Grammar 2

V-たばかりです

"Just did V"

🔍 What does it mean?

This expression indicates that not much time has passed since something happened. It conveys the feeling of "I just did ~" or "I only recently did ~." It emphasizes the recency of the action from the speaker's perspective.

📐 Formation
V-た (past form) + ばかりです

Connect ばかりです directly to the た-form of the verb. The verb stays in past tense.

Dictionary Form た-Form ＋ ばかりです Meaning
来くる 来きた 来きたばかりです just came
着つく 着ついた 着ついたばかりです just arrived
帰かえる 帰かえった 帰かえったばかりです just went back
始はじめる 始はじめた 始はじめたばかりです just started
食たべる 食たべた 食たべたばかりです just ate
💬 Dialogue Example
A：合気道あいきどうは長ながいんですか？
Have you been doing aikido for a long time?
B：いいえ、最近さいきん、始はじめたばかりです。
No, I just started recently.
📝 Example
さっき空港くうこうに着ついたばかりです。
I just arrived at the airport not too long ago.
💡 Key Insight: The word ばかり itself means "only" or "nothing but." In this pattern, V-たばかり conveys the nuance that the action happened so recently that it feels like "nothing but that moment" has passed. Note that "recently" is subjective — 先週せんしゅう来きたばかりです ("I just came last week") is perfectly natural if a week still feels recent to the speaker.

MNN L7 もう V-ましたか / まだです

In MNN Lesson 7, you learned もう～ましたか ("Have you already ~?") and the response まだです ("Not yet"). The ～たばかりです pattern adds a new dimension: it doesn't just say you did something, but that you did it very recently. Compare: もう食たべました ("I already ate") vs. 食たべたばかりです ("I just ate").

MNN L19 V-た form (た-form basics)

The た-form you learned in MNN Lesson 19 is the foundation for this grammar point. Make sure you can confidently conjugate all verb groups into た-form before using ～たばかりです.

Grammar 3

V-ていました

Past Habitual / Continuous

🔍 What does it mean?

You already know V-ています from Irodori Book 1 (初級しょきゅう1), which describes a current ongoing state or habit — for example, 介護かいごの仕事しごとをしています ("I work as a caregiver") or いけばなを習ならっています ("I am learning flower arrangement").

V-ていました is simply the past tense of V-ています. It describes something you used to do or were doing at a certain point in the past. It's used here specifically to talk about past jobs or past habitual activities in your home country.

📐 Formation
V-て + いました

Same as V-ています, but change います → いました for past tense.

🟢 Present (V-ています)
ホテルで働はたらいています。
I work at a hotel. (now)
🔵 Past (V-ていました)
ホテルで働はたらいていました。
I used to work at a hotel.
💬 Dialogue Example
A：国くにではどんな仕事しごとをしていましたか？
What kind of work did you do back home?
B：ツアーガイドの仕事しごとをしていました。
I worked as a tour guide.
💡 Key Insight: Don't confuse V-ていました with V-ました. While both are past tense, V-ました describes a one-time completed action ("I worked yesterday"), whereas V-ていました emphasizes a continued state or habit over a period of time ("I used to work there / I was working there for a while").

MNN L14 V-ています (present progressive/state)

In MNN Lesson 14, you learned V-ています to express ongoing actions and states: 今いま、食たべています ("I am eating now") and 結婚けっこんしています ("I am married"). The V-ていました form is simply the past version of this same pattern.

MNN L15 V-ています (habitual action)

MNN Lesson 15 expanded V-ています to describe habitual actions: 毎日まいにち走はしっています ("I run every day"). V-ていました describes past habits: 毎日まいにち走はしっていました ("I used to run every day").

Grammar 4

それ / そこ

文脈指示ぶんみゃくしじ — Discourse Demonstratives

🔍 What does it mean?

You already know それ ("that thing") and そこ ("that place") from basic Japanese, where they point to something physically near the listener. But in this lesson, they serve a new role: discourse demonstratives. This means they refer back to something already mentioned in the conversation, not something you can physically see.

Think of it like using "that" or "there" in English to refer to something you just talked about: "I went to Yogyakarta. It's about 4 hours from there by bus."

📐 Two Uses of それ/そこ
👉 Physical (near listener)

それ、食たべてもいいですか？
May I eat that?

💬 Discourse (previously mentioned)

それは何なんですか？
What is that? (referring to something just said)

📝 Key Example
ジャワ島とうに、ジョグジャカルタという大おおきい町まちがあります。ウォノソボは、そこから、バスで４時間じかんぐらいです。
There is a large city called Yogyakarta on the island of Java. Wonosobo is about four hours by bus from there (= from Yogyakarta).
💬 Dialogue Example
A：私わたしの町まちは、ストロマトライトが有名ゆうめいです。
My town is famous for stromatolites.
B：それは何なんですか？
What is that? (= What are stromatolites?)
💡 Key Insight: When you hear それ or そこ in conversation and there's nothing physically being pointed at, think: "What was just mentioned?" That's the referent. This discourse use of こそあど is very common in natural Japanese storytelling and conversation.

MNN L2 これ / それ / あれ (physical demonstratives)

In MNN Lesson 2, you learned the basic こそあど system for pointing at physical objects: これ (near me), それ (near you), あれ (far from both). Now, それ/そこ gain a new meaning in discourse — pointing back at previously mentioned information rather than physical objects.

MNN L3 ここ / そこ / あそこ (places)

MNN Lesson 3 taught そこ as "there (near you)." In discourse, そこ means "that place (the one I just mentioned)" — e.g., ジョグジャカルタ...そこから ("from there" = from Yogyakarta).

Grammar 5a

～て、～

Listing & Linking Sentences

🔍 What does it mean?

The ～て form is used to simply connect two sentences together, listing facts or events without implying a cause-effect relationship. Think of it as the Japanese equivalent of "and" when joining two descriptive statements.

📐 Formation
Sentence 1 ～て 、Sentence 2

For い-adjectives: おいしくて　|　For な-adjectives/nouns: しずかで　|　For verbs: V-て form

📝 Example
町まちの中心ちゅうしんは、屋台やたいがたくさんあって、にぎやかです。
The city center has many food stands, and it is bustling.
💡 Key Insight: The ～て connection is the most neutral of the three connectors. It doesn't explain why or contrast — it simply lists. Use it when both facts stand equally side by side, like describing features of a place: "There are parks, and it's quiet."

MNN L16 V-て、V-て、V-ます (sequential actions)

In MNN Lesson 16, you learned to chain actions: 朝あさ起おきて、シャワーを浴あびて、学校がっこうへ行いきます ("I wake up, take a shower, and go to school"). This lesson extends ～て to connect descriptions of places and things, not just sequential actions.

MNN L8 い-adjective + な-adjective basics

The adjective conjugations from MNN Lesson 8 (おおきい → おおきくて, しずかな → しずかで) are the building blocks for this connector pattern.

Grammar 5b

～から、～

Cause & Reason

🔍 What does it mean?

Unlike ～て which neutrally lists, ～から explains a reason or cause. It connects Sentence 1 (the reason) with Sentence 2 (the result). Think of it as "because" or "since."

📐 Formation
Reason ～から 、Result

Works with polite form (首都しゅとですから) and plain form (首都しゅとだから).

📝 Example
町まちは山やまの中なかだから、空気くうきがよくて、とても気持きもちがいいんです。
Because the town is in the mountains, the air is clean and it feels very refreshing.
📝 Example
ウランバートルは首都しゅとだから、都会とかいですよ。
Because Ulaanbaatar is the capital, it's an urban city, you know.
💡 Key Insight: Notice the example above uses both ～から and ～て together! Japanese often chains these connectors: "Because the town is in the mountains (山やまの中なかだから), the air is good (空気くうきがよくて) AND it feels refreshing (気持きもちがいい)."

MNN L9 ～から (reason)

You first encountered ～から in MNN Lesson 9: 時間じかんがありませんから、朝あさごはんを食たべません ("Because I don't have time, I don't eat breakfast"). This lesson reviews the same grammar but applies it to describing places and combining with ～て and ～けど.

Grammar 5c

～けど、～

Contrast / "But"

🔍 What does it mean?

～けど connects two sentences that are in contrast with each other. It works like "but," "although," or "however." Use it when the second sentence presents an unexpected or opposing fact compared to the first.

📐 Formation
Sentence 1 ～けど 、Sentence 2 (contrast)

Works with polite form (首都しゅとですけど) and plain form (首都しゅとだけど). Can also connect negative + positive or positive + negative sentences.

📝 Example 1
私わたしの町まちは有名ゆうめいじゃないけど、とてもいい町まちです。
My town is not famous, but it is a very nice town.
📝 Example 2
首都しゅとではないけど、とてもにぎやかです。
It is not the capital, but it is very lively.

📊 Comparing All Three Connectors

🔵 ～て (Listing / "and")

屋台やたいがたくさんあって、にぎやかです。

There are many stalls and it's lively.

🟢 ～から (Reason / "because")

首都しゅとだから、都会とかいです。

Because it's the capital, it's urban.

🟡 ～けど (Contrast / "but")

有名ゆうめいじゃないけど、いい町まちです。

Not famous, but it's a nice town.

MNN L8 ～が (contrast / "but")

In MNN Lesson 8, you learned ～が as a connector meaning "but": この部屋へやは広ひろいですが、暗くらいです ("This room is spacious but dark"). ～けど is the casual equivalent of ～が. They mean the same thing, but ～けど is much more common in spoken Japanese.

MNN L9 ～から (reason)

The ～から pattern was introduced in MNN L9. This lesson brings ～て, ～から, and ～けど together as a complete toolkit for connecting sentences in descriptions.
