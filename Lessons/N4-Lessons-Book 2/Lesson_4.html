Grammar 1

N なら、～

Conditional Recommendations

🔍 What does it mean?

N なら is used to pick up on a keyword from what the other person said and respond with a recommendation or relevant information. Think of it as: "If it's N (that you're looking for), then I recommend ~." N is the keyword that echoes the listener's needs.

📐 Formation
N (keyword from listener) + なら、recommendation / suggestion
💬 Dialogue 1 — Restaurant Recommendation
A：この近ちかくに、おすすめの店みせがありますか？
Are there any restaurants you recommend around here?
B：この近ちかくなら、「平兵衛へいべえ」がおすすめです。
If it's near here (you want), I recommend "Heibee."
💬 Dialogue 2 — Specific Type
A：おいしいラーメン屋やさん、知しりませんか？
Do you know any good ramen places?
B：おいしい店みせなら、「千歩せんぽ」だね。
If it's a tasty place (you want), "Sempo" is the one.
💬 Dialogue 3 — Budget Concern
A：安やすくお昼ひるご飯はんが食たべたいんですけど…。
I'd like to eat an inexpensive lunch...
B：安やすい店みせなら、「田山たやま食堂しょくどう」かな。安やすいし、おいしいよ。
If it's a cheap place, maybe "Tayama Shokudo." It's cheap and delicious.
💡 Key Insight: N なら works by echoing a keyword from the other person's question. They say "near here" → you reply この近くなら. They say "delicious ramen" → you reply おいしい店なら. It shows you're actively listening and tailoring your recommendation to their specific need!

MNN L25 ～たら (conditional)

In MNN Lesson 25, you learned ～たら for conditional expressions: 雨あめが降ふったら、行いきません ("If it rains, I won't go"). N なら is a simpler, topic-focused conditional: instead of a full if-then clause, it picks up a single noun as the condition: ラーメンなら ("If it's ramen [you want]..."). It's conversational and natural for giving recommendations.

L3 Irodori ～のは、N です (recommendations)

In Lesson 3, you learned ～のは to present popular/recommended items. N なら adds another recommendation tool: instead of "The popular one is X," you say "If you want Y, then X is good." Both are essential for restaurant conversations.

Grammar 2

V-て、～ / V-ないで、～

Manners — How to Eat & Drink

🔍 What does it mean?

These patterns explain how to eat or drink something by describing the state or method. V-て、～ means "do V, then do ~" (in that state/manner). V-ないで、～ means "without doing V, do ~." They're essential for food manners and cooking instructions.

✅ V-て、～ (Do this, then eat)

つゆにつけて食たべてください。

Please dip it in sauce and eat it.

ドレッシングをかけて食たべてください。

Please put dressing on it and eat.

❌ V-ないで、～ (Without doing this, eat)

しょうゆをつけないで食たべてください。

Please eat it without adding soy sauce.

皮かわをむかないで、そのまま食たべられます。

You can eat it as is, without peeling.

📐 Formation
V-て、～てください  = "Do V, then do ~"

V-ないで、～てください  = "Without V-ing, do ~"

V-ないで = Verb ナイ-form + で. Example: つける → つけない → つけないで

📝 Tempura Instructions
こちらの野菜やさいの天てんぷらは、つゆにつけて食たべてください。
For these vegetable tempura, please dip them in the sauce and eat.
📝 Shrimp Instructions
エビはつゆにつけないで、塩しおをかけて食たべてください。
For the shrimp, please don't dip in sauce — sprinkle salt and eat instead.
💡 Key Insight: The V-て form here describes the manner or method of eating, not a sequence of separate actions. つけて食べる = "eat by dipping" (one combined action). This is different from V-てから (Grammar 4) which means "after doing V, then do ~" (two separate sequential actions).

MNN L14 V-て form basics

You first learned the て-form in MNN L14 for requests: 窓まどを開あけてください. This lesson uses the same て-form to describe how to do something: つけて食べる ("eat by dipping").

MNN L17 V-ないでください (negative requests)

MNN L17 taught V-ないでください: 写真しゃしんを撮とらないでください ("Please don't take photos"). V-ないで here drops ください and means "without V-ing": つけないで食べる ("eat without dipping").

Grammar 3

V-ちゃだめです

Casual Prohibition — "You shouldn't ~"

🔍 What does it mean?

V-ちゃだめです is a casual, spoken way to say "you shouldn't do ~" or "don't do ~." It's the contracted form of V-てはだめです. You'll hear this when someone warns you about a wrong way to eat or cook something.

📐 Formation
V-て form → drop て → add ちゃだめです

Special case: When the て-form is ～んで (e.g. 飲のんで, 泳およいで), it becomes ～じゃ: 飲んじゃだめ, 泳いじゃだめ

FormalCasual (Contracted)Meaning
入いれてはだめです入いれちゃだめですDon't put in ~
かけてはだめですかけちゃだめですDon't pour on ~
食たべてはだめです食たべちゃだめですDon't eat ~
飲のんではだめです飲のんじゃだめですDon't drink ~
泳およいではだめです泳およいじゃだめですDon't swim
📝 Example 1
一度いちどに、そんなにたくさんお肉にくを入いれちゃだめですよ。
Don't put in so much meat at once!
📝 Example 2
そんなにたくさんしょうゆをかけちゃだめですよ。
Don't add so much soy sauce!
💬 Dialogue — Old Food
A：あっ、これはもう古ふるいから、食たべちゃだめです。
Oh, this is old, so don't eat it.
B：そうなんですか。
Is that so?
💡 Key Rule for ちゃ vs. じゃ: If the て-form ends in ～て → use ちゃ (食べて → 食べちゃ). If the て-form ends in ～で → use じゃ (飲んで → 飲んじゃ). This contraction pattern (ては→ちゃ, では→じゃ) appears everywhere in casual Japanese!

MNN L15 V-てはいけません (prohibition)

In MNN L15, you learned the formal prohibition: 写真しゃしんを撮とってはいけません ("You must not take photos"). V-ちゃだめです is the casual spoken version. てはいけません (formal/written) → てはだめです (semi-formal) → ちゃだめ (casual spoken).

MNN L14 て-form conjugation

The て-form patterns from MNN L14 are essential: 食べる→食べて (Group 2), 飲む→飲んで (Group 1). Remember that verbs with んで in the て-form use じゃ, not ちゃ.

Grammar 4

V-てから、～

Sequential Actions — "After doing ~"

🔍 What does it mean?

V-てから clearly states that one action happens first, and then the next action follows after it. It emphasizes the order of two distinct actions: "Do V first, and then ~." This is essential for cooking instructions where the sequence matters!

📐 Formation
V-て form + から、next action

"First do V, and then (after that) do the next action."

📝 Example 1 — Hotpot Cooking
うどんは、お肉にくと野菜やさいを食たべてから、入いれましょう。
Let's add the udon after eating the meat and vegetables.
📝 Example 2 — Okonomiyaki
お好このみ焼やきは最初さいしょに具ぐをよく混まぜてから、焼やきます。
For okonomiyaki, mix the ingredients well first, then cook it.

⚖️ V-て、～ vs. V-てから、～

Both can describe sequences, but the nuance differs significantly:

🥢 V-て、～ (Manner/Method)

つゆにつけて食たべる

"Eat by dipping in sauce"
(one combined action — the manner of eating)

⏱️ V-てから、～ (Then/After)

混まぜてから、焼やく

"Mix first, then cook"
(two separate actions in clear order)

💡 Key Insight: V-てから emphasizes "FIRST do this, THEN do that" — the order is the whole point. V-て simply describes manner/method or loosely connects actions. For cooking instructions where getting the order wrong ruins the dish, V-てから is the right choice!

MNN L16 V-てから (sequence)

MNN L16 first introduced V-てから: 家いえに帰かえってから、晩ばんご飯はんを食たべます ("After going home, I eat dinner"). This lesson applies the same pattern to cooking instructions: "After mixing, cook" / "After eating the meat, add noodles."

Grammar 5

S1 が、S2

Formal Contrast — "but"

🔍 What does it mean?

～が connects two contrasting sentences, similar to "but" in English. It is the more formal and written version of ～けど, which you likely use in casual speech already. You'll see ～が in restaurant reviews, formal descriptions, and polite conversation.

📐 Formation
S1 (polite form) + が、S2

が connects to the polite form (～ます / ～です), making it suitable for formal contexts.

📝 Example 1 — Restaurant Review
お店みせは新あたらしくてきれいですが、お昼ひるは混こんでいます。
The restaurant is new and clean, but it's crowded at lunch.
📝 Example 2
料理りょうりはとてもおいしかったですが、少すこし値段ねだんが高たかかったです。
The food was very tasty, but it was a bit expensive.
📝 が — Formal / Written

おいしかったですが、高たかかったです。

Reviews, formal speech, writing

💬 けど — Casual / Spoken

おいしかったけど、高たかかった。

Casual conversation, texting

💡 Key Insight: が and けど both mean "but," with the same grammatical function. The difference is purely register (formality level). Use が in writing, reviews, polite conversations, and formal settings. Use けど in everyday casual speech. They are not interchangeable in tone — mixing them up sounds unnatural.

MNN L5 ～が、～ (contrast/introduction)

In MNN very early lessons, you used が to introduce topics politely: すみませんが、駅えきはどこですか ("Excuse me, but where is the station?"). This lesson's が is the same particle used specifically for contrasting two facts: "The food was good, but it was expensive."

Irodori L5 初級1 ～けど (casual contrast)

In 初級しょきゅう1, you learned けど for contrast in casual speech. が is the formal upgrade: same meaning, higher register. For restaurant reviews and polite comments, が is the natural choice.

B：おいしかったですが、ちょっと量りょうが多おおかったです。 ← S1が、S2
It was delicious, but the portions were a bit too much.

Bonus

🔑 Casual Contraction Guide

ては→ちゃ / では→じゃ

📊 The ちゃ / じゃ Contraction System

The contraction ては → ちゃ and では → じゃ appears in many casual Japanese patterns beyond just ちゃだめ. Understanding this system will help you recognize casual speech everywhere.

Formal PatternCasual ContractionMeaning
V-てはだめV-ちゃだめYou shouldn't do V
V-ではだめV-じゃだめYou shouldn't do V (んで verbs)
V-てしまうV-ちゃうEnd up doing V (unintended)
V-でしまうV-じゃうEnd up doing V (んで verbs)
V-ているV-てるIs doing V (progressive)
💡 Pro Tip: Whenever you see ちゃ in casual speech, mentally convert it back to ては to understand the grammar. 食べちゃだめ = 食べてはだめ. 食べちゃった = 食べてしまった. Once you spot this pattern, casual Japanese becomes much easier to parse!
