Grammar 1

S1 ので、S2

Giving Reasons Politely

🔍 What does it mean?

ので is used to give a reason or explanation for something, especially in a polite and soft way. In this lesson, it's specifically used to explain why you can't eat or drink something — for example, when declining food due to allergies, diet, or personal reasons at a restaurant.

📐 Formation (Plain Form Connection)
S1 (reason — plain form) + ので、S2 (result)

For ナ-adjectives and Nouns: ～な + ので  |  For イ-adjectives and Verbs: plain form + ので

Type Polite Plain + ので Example
ナ-adj ベジタリアンです ベジタリアンなので ベジタリアンなので、肉にくはだめなんです。
Noun 有名ゆうめいです 有名ゆうめいなので 有名ゆうめいなので、いつも混こんでいます。
イ-adj 安やすいです 安やすいので 安やすいので、よく来きます。
Verb 自転車じてんしゃで来きました 自転車じてんしゃで来きたので 自転車じてんしゃで来きたので、飲のめないんです。
📝 Example 1
今日きょうは自転車じてんしゃで来きたので、飲のめないんです。
Because I came by bicycle today, I can't drink (alcohol).
📝 Example 2
ベジタリアンなので、肉にくとか魚さかなはだめなんです。
Because I'm a vegetarian, meat and fish are no good for me.

⚖️ ので vs. から — What's the difference?

🟢 ので — Softer, More Polite

ベジタリアンなので、食たべられません。

Objective, gentle explanation. Best for formal settings, speaking to superiors, and delicate situations like declining food.

🟡 から — More Direct

ベジタリアンだから、食たべられません。

More personal, direct tone. Fine with friends, but can sound blunt in formal settings.

💡 Key Insight: When giving personal reasons for declining food or drink (allergies, diet, lifestyle), ので is the preferred choice because it sounds softer and less confrontational. It frames the reason as an objective fact rather than a personal assertion. Think of it as the "professional" version of から.

MNN L9 ～から (giving reasons)

In MNN Lesson 9, you learned ～から for giving reasons: 時間じかんがありませんから、朝あさごはんを食たべません. ので is the softer alternative to から. Both mean "because," but ので sounds more polite and objective. In formal or sensitive situations (like explaining allergies), ので is strongly preferred.

MNN L20 Plain form (普通形)

ので connects to the plain form, which you studied in MNN Lesson 20. The key difference: for ナ-adjectives and nouns, you use な (not だ) before ので: しずかなので (not しずかだので).

Grammar 2

S よね

Confirming What You Believe

🔍 What does it mean?

よね combines two particles you've already seen: よ (asserting information) and ね (seeking agreement). Together, よね means "I believe X is true — you agree, right?" It's used when you think something is the case and want to confirm it with the other person.

📐 Formation
Statement + よね

The speaker thinks the statement is true but wants the listener to confirm.

🆚 よね vs. ですか — What's the difference?

💬 よね — "It is X, right?"

おすし、わさび、入はいってますよね。

This sushi has wasabi in it, doesn't it?
(You already think it does — just confirming)

❓ ですか — "Is it X?"

おすし、わさび、入はいってますか？

Does this sushi have wasabi in it?
(You genuinely don't know and are asking)

💬 Dialogue — At a Restaurant
A：定食ていしょくのご飯はんは、おかわりできますよね。
You can get free refills of rice with the set meal, right?
B：はい、できますよ。
Yes, you can.
💡 Key Insight: よね is extremely useful at restaurants! You can use it to confirm things on the menu, check about ingredients (allergens!), or verify restaurant policies. It's much smoother than asking a blunt yes/no question because it shows you've already done some thinking: "I believe this is the case — am I right?"

MNN L4 ～ね (confirmation/agreement)

You learned ね in MNN Lesson 4 for seeking agreement: いい天気てんきですね ("Nice weather, isn't it?"). よね builds on this: ね alone assumes shared knowledge, while よね adds the nuance of "I'm asserting this and asking you to confirm."

Lesson 2 よ (giving new information)

In the previous Irodori lesson, you learned よ for providing new info: あの人ひとですよ. Now よ + ね = よね: "I believe this (よ) — you agree (ね)?"

Grammar 3

N で～

Selecting & Choosing

🔍 What does it mean?

When asked to choose between options at a restaurant (seating, portion size, menu items), you answer with N で to communicate your choice. Think of it as "I'll go with N" or "N, please." It's a compact, natural way to tell staff your preference.

📐 Formation
N (your choice) + で + { お願ねがいします / だいじょうぶです / いいです }

N で can also be used alone: じゃあ、それで。("Okay, I'll go with that.")

💬 Dialogue 1 — Choosing a Seat
A：テーブルと座敷ざしきがございますが…。
We have table and tatami seating...
B：テーブルでお願ねがいします。
Table seating, please.
💬 Dialogue 2 — Portion Size
A：ご飯はんの量りょう、大盛おおもりにしますか？
Would you like an extra-large portion of rice?
B：普通ふつうでだいじょうぶです。
A normal portion is fine.
💬 Dialogue 3 — Menu Recommendation
A：ミックスフライ定食ていしょくがおすすめです。
The assorted fried food set meal is recommended.
B：じゃあ、それで。
Okay, I'll go with that.
💡 Key Insight: The で particle here functions as a "selection marker" — it indicates the item you're choosing from available options. You'll often hear it paired with polite endings like お願ねがいします ("please") or casual responses like いいです ("that's fine"). The ultra-compact じゃあ、それで is very common in casual ordering.

MNN L3 ～をください (ordering)

In MNN Lesson 3, you learned to order with ～をください ("Give me ~, please"). N で adds another ordering tool: instead of requesting a specific item, you're selecting from offered options. Compare: コーヒーをください (ordering coffee) vs. コーヒーでお願いしますおねがいします (choosing coffee from options given).

MNN L5 で particle (means/method)

In MNN Lesson 5, you learned で for means: バスで行いきます ("I go by bus"). The で in N で～ at restaurants is related — it marks your choice as the "means" by which you'll proceed with your order.

Grammar 4

～のは、N です

Nominalization for Recommendations

🔍 What does it mean?

This expression uses の (nominalizer) to turn a verb or adjective phrase into a noun-like topic, then identifies what that thing is. The pattern ～のは、N です means something like "The one that is ~/The thing that ~ is N." In restaurants, it's used to answer questions about popular dishes, recommendations, or what's delicious.

📐 Formation
ナA-な + のは、N です
イA-い + のは、N です
V-る (plain) + のは、N です

The phrase before のは sets up the precondition or description; N is the answer.

📝 Example 1 — What's popular?
人気にんきがあるのは、お刺身さしみ定食ていしょくです。
The popular one is the sashimi set meal.
📝 Example 2 — What's delicious?
この店みせでおいしいのは、親子おやこ丼どんですよ。
What's delicious at this restaurant is the oyako-don (chicken & egg rice bowl).
💬 Dialogue — Asking for a Recommendation
A：何なにがおいしいですか？
What is delicious here?
B：この店みせでおいしいのは、親子おやこ丼どんですよ。
What's delicious at this restaurant is the oyako-don.
📝 Example 3 — What's popular?
よくみんなが注文ちゅうもんするのは、ミックスフライ定食ていしょくです。
What everyone often orders is the assorted fried food set meal.
💡 Key Insight: Think of の as a "thing" or "one." おいしいの = "the delicious one / the thing that's delicious." 人気にんきがあるの = "the one that's popular." Then は marks it as the topic, and N です provides the answer. It's a very elegant way to present information — much more natural than saying 親子丼はおいしいです ("Oyako-don is delicious") when someone asks "What should I try?"

MNN L22 Noun modification (plain form + noun)

In MNN Lesson 22, you learned to modify nouns with plain-form clauses: おいしい料理りょうり ("delicious food"). The ～のは pattern takes this one step further by using の to nominalize the entire clause: おいしいの = "the delicious one / the thing that is delicious." This creates a flexible, topic-comment structure.

MNN L25 ～たら / ～ても (conditionals)

By MNN L25, you've mastered complex sentence structures. The ～のは、N です pattern continues this progression by letting you create sophisticated answers like よくみんなが注文ちゅうもんするのは ("What everyone often orders is…").

🔑 Sentence Endings: よ vs. ね vs. よね

📊 Complete Comparison — All Three Particles

Across Lessons 2 and 3, you've now learned all three major sentence-ending particles. Here's how they work together:

Particle Who Knows? Function Example
よ Speaker knows ✓
Listener doesn't ✗		 Giving NEW information or alerting あの人ひとですよ。
"It's that person." (FYI!)
ね Both know ✓✓ Seeking agreement / confirming SHARED info いい天気てんきですね。
"Nice weather, right?"
よね Speaker thinks ✓
Wants confirmation		 Asserting belief + asking to confirm わさび入はいってますよね。
"It has wasabi, doesn't it?"
💡 Restaurant Context: At a restaurant, you'll naturally use all three. よ when telling a friend about a dish ("This is really good, you know!"). ね when agreeing about the food ("This is delicious, isn't it!"). よね when confirming with staff ("You can get refills, right?").
